cult / how it works

tl;dr

You give us a cultural work. We turn it into a vector. We find a short, signed combination of more famous works whose vectors sum to roughly the same place. The result reads like an equation, e.g.

The Wire ≈ Breaking Bad + The Sopranos − Game of Thrones

Everything runs in your browser. No server, no API key.

The pipeline

Five stages, all client-side:

  1. Curate. A static list of ~750 ubiquitously famous works ships with the page (movies, books, TV, games, comics, manga, albums, musicals, franchises).
  2. Embed the basis. On first visit, the browser downloads a small embedding model and turns each title into a 384-dimensional unit vector.
  3. Cache. The resulting matrix (~1.1 MB) is stored in IndexedDB. Future visits skip stages 2–3.
  4. Embed the query. Your input goes through the same model.
  5. Decompose. A greedy sparse-coding pass picks a handful of basis vectors (with positive or negative signs) whose weighted sum lands closest to your query.

The model

We use Xenova/all-MiniLM-L6-v2 via transformers.js. It's a sentence-transformer trained to map similar phrases to nearby points on the unit sphere.

The model loads from jsDelivr's ESM CDN and runs entirely in WASM/WebGPU. After the first visit the browser caches both the weights and our pre-computed basis matrix; nothing leaves your device.

The basis

The dictionary is hand-picked for name recognition rather than orthogonality. Roughly:

  • 221 movies — from Casablanca to Oppenheimer
  • 172 books — scripture, plays, novels, kids' books
  • 142 TV shows — sitcoms, prestige drama, anime
  • 120 video games — arcade classics through Elden Ring
  • 33 albums, 30 comics, 20 musicals, 12 franchises

The bias is intentional: the goal is decompositions that read well. "Severance ≈ Black Mirror + The Office" is funnier and more useful than the same answer with three obscure short stories you've never heard of.

The math

Given a query vector q and a dictionary of N unit-norm basis vectors b₁ … b_N, we run matching pursuit: a classic greedy sparse-coding algorithm.

# initial residual is the query itself
r ← q
chosen ← []

# up to 4 iterations
for step in 1..K:
    # find the basis vector most aligned with the residual
    i* ← argmax_i |⟨r, b_i⟩|
    c  ← ⟨r, b_i*⟩          # signed coefficient

    # subtract its contribution from the residual
    r ← r − c · b_i*
    chosen.append((i*, c))

    # block near-duplicates so we don't pick "Star Wars" twice
    block all j where ⟨b_i*, b_j⟩ > 0.85

Three small twists make the output readable:

  • Self-block. Any basis vector with cosine similarity to the query above 0.92 is excluded — otherwise typing "Star Wars" just returns "Star Wars".
  • Diversity-block. After picking b_i, every other basis vector closer than 0.85 to it is also blocked, preventing redundant terms like "The Avengers" plus "Avengers: Endgame".
  • Min-coefficient stop. The greedy loop quits early when the best remaining |⟨r, b_i⟩| falls below 0.12. Some queries decompose cleanly in 2 terms; others need 4.

Reading the output

The equation

Terms are sorted by absolute coefficient (strongest contributor first). A + means the basis vector points in the same direction as the residual at that step; a − means it points opposite.

Hover any term to see its category and exact coefficient. Click a term to decompose that work next.

Cosine fit

The fit % reports how close the reconstructed vector Σ cᵢ · b_i is to your query, in cosine similarity. 100% would mean a perfect linear combination; in practice 60–85% is typical and makes for the best-reading equations.

What "−" actually means

Subtraction is the model saying: "your title is a lot like X but minus the Y-ness of it." Useful negatives often capture genre or tone differences — e.g. a slow horror film might come out as The Shining minus Psycho (slow horror ≈ canonical horror minus jump-scare snappiness).

Honest limitations

  • Title-only embeddings. The model only sees the title string, not plot, cast, or year. "Alien" and "Aliens" land in nearly the same place; "It" gets confused with the pronoun.
  • English-centric. The model was trained primarily on English. Romanized titles work but original-script titles (e.g. 千と千尋の神隠し) won't.
  • The basis is a curated list. A truly obscure or brand-new work has no chance of being in the dictionary, only of being expressed in terms of the dictionary.
  • Greedy is not optimal. Matching pursuit is fast and usually good but can miss combinations that would only appear via joint optimization (orthogonal matching pursuit, lasso, etc.). For a silly app this is fine.
  • Names mislead embeddings. "Twilight" the vampire saga and "Twilight" the Pokémon move land at the same point. There's no disambiguation.

Why?

Cultural recommendation engines tend to give you nearest neighbors: "if you liked X, try Y." This is the inverse — given the work, can you describe it as a sentence in the language of things everyone already knows? The output is closer to a critic's opening line than a recommendation.

It's also a quick test of an underappreciated property of embedding spaces: they're approximately additive over short distances. Pop culture happens to have enough mass and structure that you can do meaningful arithmetic on it.
