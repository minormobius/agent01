🌀 Fractalize — what is this?

Fractalize grows a fractal out of your photograph. The photo isn't pasted on top — it's woven into the fractal's structure, so its colours and shapes reappear along the filaments and spirals.

How a fractal is made here

Every pixel on screen is a point c on the complex plane. We repeat a simple rule — for the Mandelbrot set, z → z² + c — over and over and watch what the running value z does:

  • If z stays bounded forever, the pixel is inside the set.
  • If z flies off to infinity, the pixel escapes — and how quickly it escapes is what classic fractal colouring is built on.

The other types change the rule slightly: Julia fixes c and lets the starting point vary; Burning Ship takes the absolute value of z each step; Tricorn conjugates it; Multibrot raises z to a higher power. The power slider even allows fractional exponents.

Where your photo comes in: orbit traps

As z bounces around during the iteration, it traces a path — an orbit. We watch how close that orbit comes to a chosen shape (a point, a line, a cross, a circle) and remember the spot of closest approach. That spot is then used as a coordinate to look up a colour in your photograph.

Because nearby pixels have orbits that graze the trap at slightly different places, the photo gets stretched and folded along the fractal's natural contours. This is what makes the image become the fractal rather than sit behind it.

Colour modes

  • Photo · orbit trap — sample the photo at the orbit's closest approach to the trap. The most "painterly" mode.
  • Photo + palette hybrid — blend the orbit-trap photo with a gradient built from the photo's own dominant colours.
  • Photo · final z — sample the photo at the orbit's final position instead of its closest approach. Wilder, more scattered.
  • Iteration palette — classic escape-time colouring, but the gradient is extracted from your photo by median-cut.

The controls, group by group

Photograph — load an image, then optionally drag a rectangle on the preview to feed only a section of it into the fractal. The swatch strip shows the extracted palette; the histogram shows tone distribution.

Fractal — type, power, iteration count (higher = more detail in thin filaments, but slower), and escape radius (larger radii smooth the colour bands).

Julia seed — only matters for Julia type. Nudge c.re/c.im or click pick seed and then click anywhere on the canvas to lift a seed straight off the plane.

View — drag to pan, scroll to zoom toward the cursor, rotate, reset.

Coloring — the heart of the look. Trap shape and position decide which part of the orbit drives the image; image scale / rotate / offset control how the photo maps onto the orbit space; palette shift & density cycle the gradient; mix balances photo against palette / interior.

Post — brightness, contrast, saturation, gamma, applied after everything.

Export — render at up to 4× screen resolution to a PNG. Randomize look reshuffles the colouring controls for happy accidents.

Tips

  • Zoom into the boundary of the set — the busy edges are where orbit traps put the most photo detail.
  • High-contrast photos with a few bold colours read best.
  • Crop to a face or a single object, then set a point trap and small image scale for a kaleidoscopic portrait.
  • Low iterations + large escape radius gives soft, smoky colour; high iterations gives crisp, threadlike structure.

Privacy & tech

Everything runs in your browser on the GPU (WebGL). Your photo never leaves the device — there's no upload and no server.

Part of a small series of photo experiments: /astro (read a photo's sky by its timestamp) · /prism (the camera through a prismatic cornea).
