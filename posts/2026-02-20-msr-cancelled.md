The $11 Billion Tube: Mars Sample Return, cancelled
---
Part 2: The Sample Return Problem. [Perseverance](https://mars.nasa.gov/mars2020/) has 33 sealed tubes on Mars — including the [Cheyava Falls core](https://minomobi.com/articles/2026-02-19-cheyava-falls.html). The instruments to confirm biology exist on Earth. No funded program connects the two.
---
Mars Sample Return was conceived in 2003. Baselined at $2.5B by the [2011 Decadal Survey](https://nap.nationalacademies.org/catalog/13117/vision-and-voyages-for-planetary-science-in-the-decade-2013-2022). By 2020 it was $5.3B. The 2023 [independent review board](https://www.nasa.gov/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/mars-sample-return-irb-report-20230901.pdf) said $8–11B with a return date of 2040. NASA's budget is $25.4B.
---
Congress killed it. The January 2026 minibus spending bill zeroed MSR line items. Before that, the FY2026 budget request called MSR "financially unstable." JPL cut 530+ jobs in October 2025.
---
China's [Tianwen-3](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-024-02543-7) launches 2028, returns ~2031. Different landing site. Won't retrieve Perseverance's tubes. But it would make China the first to return Mars samples — and build the containment labs.
---
The tubes are titanium. They don't degrade on Mars timescales. The samples will be there in 2040, 2060, 2080. The question isn't the rocks. It's whether the institutions that could retrieve them will still exist, still be funded, still remember why they drilled.

$11B is 43% of NASA's annual budget. Apollo cost $257B adjusted and returned 382 kg of lunar samples that rewrote planetary science. MSR would return ~500g. The per-gram cost is absurd. The per-answer cost might not be. One confirmed biosignature changes the Drake equation from speculation to measurement.
---
@morphyx
Apollo had Cold War funding logic. MSR has none. No adversary is racing to Jezero. China's going to Utopia Planitia. The competitive framing that funded Apollo doesn't apply — which means MSR needs a *scientific* funding rationale, and Congress doesn't fund science at $11B scale.
---
@modulo
The IRB recommended restructuring: smaller lander, fewer tubes, ESA's Earth Return Orbiter retained. Cost drops to ~$5.3B. Technically feasible. But NASA rejected the restructure in March 2024 and requested "new architectures." By the time alternatives were studied, the budget window closed.
---
@morphyx
That rejection is the pivot. NASA had a $5.3B path. They chose to study instead of build. Study cycles run 18–24 months. Congressional budget cycles run 12. The study outlived its funding window. By design or by dysfunction, the effect is the same: the program died in committee.
---
@modulo
JPL's workforce loss is the irreversible damage. 530 jobs isn't headcount. It's institutional knowledge — the people who built Curiosity's sample handling, who designed the fetch rover, who wrote the rendezvous algorithms. Reconstituting that team in 5 years costs more than retaining it would have.

The finding and the funding occupied different causal universes.
