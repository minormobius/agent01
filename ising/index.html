the ising model · spontaneous magnetization · lenz 1920 · onsager 1944

↑ geometry pack · sister of traffic — the other phase transition you can hear

A sheet of tiny magnets — spins, each pointing up or down. Every spin wants to agree with its four neighbours; heat keeps knocking them out of line. Turn the temperature down and the whole sheet spontaneously magnetizes — one opinion sweeps the lattice with nobody in charge. Turn it up past a critical point and the order melts into noise. And because we wired loudness to temperature, you can drop in a track and watch the music melt and re-freeze the magnet — then pull an 8-bit soundtrack back out of it.

|magnetization| vs time
up down. |Magnetization| is the net alignment — the order parameter. Cold: it locks near 1 (one colour wins). Hot: it hovers near 0 (salt-and-pepper). Near Tc it shimmers, with domains of every size at once.

The order parameter is the steady-state |magnetization|. Sweep the temperature up and it holds near 1 (fully ordered) until the critical temperature Tc = 2/ln(1+√2) ≈ 2.269, where it bends down to 0 — Onsager's exact curve, |M| ~ (Tc−T)1/8. Unlike traffic's cliff this is a continuous transition, but the knee at Tc is unmistakable.

The rule

The Ising model is a grid of spins si = ±1 on an L × L torus. The energy rewards agreement between neighbours and alignment with an external field H:

E = −J Σ⟨ij⟩ sisj − H Σi si

with coupling J = 1 (ferromagnetic — neighbours want to match). We evolve it with the Metropolis algorithm: pick a spin, compute the energy change ΔE of flipping it, and flip with probability min(1, e−ΔE/T). Flips that lower the energy always happen; flips that raise it happen sometimes, more often when it's hot. That single rule is the whole thermostat. One sweep is L×L flip attempts.

Spontaneous magnetization

At high temperature the noise wins: spins point every which way, |magnetization| ≈ 0, the sheet is a paramagnet. Cool it, and the neighbour-coupling starts to win — patches of agreement grow, merge, and below a sharp critical temperature Tc a single orientation spontaneously takes the entire lattice even with no external field. The symmetry between up and down breaks on its own.

Nobody tells the spins which way to point. Below Tc they simply agree — that's a ferromagnet, and it's why a horseshoe magnet stays magnetized.

In two dimensions Lars Onsager solved it exactly in 1944: the transition sits at Tc = 2 / ln(1 + √2) ≈ 2.269, and just below it the magnetization rises as |M| ~ (Tc − T)1/8 — that 1/8 is a critical exponent, the same for every system in the model's universality class, magnets and liquids and alloys alike. Hear it — and sonify it

We mapped loudness → temperature, so a loud passage heats the sheet past Tc and melts the order while a quiet one lets it re-freeze; each beat fires a brief external-field pulse that nucleates domains. Then the arrow reverses: sonify reads the magnet's own state — its order sets major-vs-minor, a swept column of spins becomes the lead (a near-drone when frozen, a scatter when molten), and the flip-rate drives the percussion (silent when cold, frantic near Tc). The track it emits is a real com.minomobi.music.composition you can export to /music — or feed straight back in and let the magnet pulse to the soundtrack it just sang.

Why it belongs in the geometry pack

The Ising model is the statistical-mechanics phase transition — the same emergent-order story as traffic's gridlock and the Markov thermostats, but here with an exact solution and a clean order parameter. Criticality, broken symmetry, universality, diverging correlation length: all of it acted out by a sheet of ±1s.
