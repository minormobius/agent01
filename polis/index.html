polis — the city cascade

Polis is the city proper. A city cannot be simulated alone: its history is a client of everything above it. So a city here runs as a cascade of three histories, each one boundary conditions for the next, each one refining the same deterministic world — because mappa carries no truth below its cells, every level mints its finer geography (coasts, rivers, biome edges recomputed at higher resolution), conditioned on the level above and seeded by the address.

the city ↗
the micro · streets · habitat-determined
first layer live — the settlement field

The city proper: districts, blocks, streets — so strongly determined by habitat that the plan falls out of the terrain: the river bend fixes the port, the defensible rise fixes the citadel, walls trace the epoch-2 fortification, district kinds come from what the civ city actually contains (its institutions rollup — a city is an org of orgs). Recursive Voronoi descent, every address a seed: world:city:cell:d2:b5:lot3.

The settlement field is live — the original polis theory's three field regimes run as one life cycle over a true Voronoi frame: founding spokes to the one engine that matters (the Physarum star), coverage capillaries sprouting toward worst-served tissue (the hypoxia solver), then the arterial hierarchy as flux between district anchors once the base diversifies. On top runs the land market (Ricardo → von Thünen → Alonso): every cell goes to the use that bids most for its access — commerce takes the core, industry the mill and port, and the farms that feed the town rise first and are displaced outward as the land grows too dear (Sinclair's development shadow empties the near fringe before the city even arrives). And the mesh itself is alive: the city is one continuous Voronoi over a growing point set, and every tick every cell holds the power of mitosis — where product (rent × area) runs high the cell divides and the whole diagram re-tessellates, seamlessly. Fine grain downtown and coarse pasture at the edge are outcomes of the land market, not settings. Walls rise on the civ tick and leave a fringe belt; sacks burn quarters that rebuild.

Arrive from a civ run: ./city/?civ=1&world=…&preset=…&city=k — the envelope, the era ticks, the walls tick and every sack arrive as boundary conditions. Blocks and plots (the Conzen burgage layer) are the next descent. The full design: STRATEGY.md, Phase VII.

Why run three histories?

Because that's how downscaling works — a regional model nested in a global one, nested again for the site. Same discipline here: each level's aggregates must track the level above (the hinterland's towns sum toward the civ city's envelope; the city's wards sum toward its town), while each level adds structure the level above cannot represent. The upper runs are cheap and content-addressed: the civ run caches forever, the hinterland runs in milliseconds — the cascade for one city costs barely more than the city itself.
