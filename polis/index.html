A theory of city development

A procedural engine that grows the whole history of a city into a generated world — founding to metropolis — deterministically, from a seed. It stands on mappa's planet and reuses two field solvers already living in hoop. The long arc is a 4X game. This page is the theory it will be built on.

The central idea ↓ ↤ mappa, the world engine Sources ↗
Status: theory / design — no engine yet. This is the charter for one.
mappa answers where, once. It generates a deterministic planet — plate tectonics, hydrology, climate, biomes — and scatters plausible city sites on it. polis answers how a city becomes one, over centuries. It takes a site and grows its streets, quarters, arteries and hinterland through the real developmental stages urban geography has documented for two hundred years — and it does so by handing one flow-solver off to another as the city ages.

00The thesis

Three things already exist in this repo, and polis is the missing middle between them:

LayerWhat it isWhat it gives polis

mappa's placement is a habitability k-means scatter — plausible dots, but with no notion of a city growing: no streets, no quarters, no centuries. A 4X needs cities with a legible history — a founding reason, an organic core, arteries that thickened with trade, a hinterland that filled in. polis is that layer, and its claim is that the history can be generated by running two solvers hoop already has, handed off from one to the other.

02The central idea — one field, two regimes

A city is always solving a flow problem on a graph. But which problem changes as it grows, and that change is the whole story. The two hoop solvers turn out to be the same operator — the graph Laplacian — with different forcing.

Coverage regime · the young town

hoop/v7/foam.js — seize()

Problem: keep everyone served — every dwelling within reach of water, market, mill, gate.

Physics: a diffusion / Poisson field. A saturation field spreads from supply points; where it falls short — the hypoxic fringe — the town sprouts a lane toward it (angiogenesis).

Form: space-filling, branching, organic. The medieval town plan.

Demand regime · the mature city

hoop/paint/flux.js — growNetwork()

Problem: move concentrated flows — thousands of journeys between many origins and destinations.

Physics: a current-flow field. Inject origin→destination current, solve for the potential; the stationary flux is the time-integrated occupancy of every street. Streets are its superlevel set.

Form: arterial, hierarchical. The trading city.

This is not a metaphor we impose — it is what the kernels already compute. flux.js's own header notes that the stationary flux of trips is the Laplace transform of the journey process (the graph's Green's function). foam.js's seize() is a multi-source BFS perfusion field plus angiogenesis toward the most under-served tissue. Same operator L; the coverage regime forces it with a near-uniform source, the demand regime with a peaked O–D current. A city ages from the first forcing to the second. Early, the binding constraint is area served; late, it is flow carried.

The same activation/inhibition tension shows up in complexity science as a Turing reaction–diffusion instability (short-range agglomeration vs long-range congestion) — the coverage/demand handoff is the discrete, graph-side cousin of that pattern-forming physics.

03The four phases

Phase 1 · why hereThe site

A settlement nucleates where the ground gives it a reason. The classic distinction is site vs situation: site is the physical spot (relief, drainage, water, defensibility); situation is its relation to the wider world (routes, resources, other settlements). They are independent — a town can have a poor site but a commanding situation, or the reverse — so polis scores every candidate cell on two separate axes. Some features are decisive enough to force a town almost regardless of spacing:

→ rule situation = betweenness on the route/river graph; site = local terrain sample. Spawn where w_site·site + w_situation·situation clears a threshold, with forced points pre-seeded. The founding advantage is a one-time tie-break, not a permanent subsidy — which is exactly what lets a city outlive the reason it was founded (Phase 4).

Phase 2 · the hypoxia phaseNucleation & organic accretion

A village becomes a town. The binding constraints are food and reach.

  • Food first — Childe's Urban Revolution (1950): a town can hold only as many non-farmers as its agricultural surplus feeds. max_specialists ≈ (arable × yield × surplus_fraction) / food_per_capita, read off mappa's fertility. No surplus → stuck at village tier.
  • Reach second — the angiogenesis solver (foam.js seize()): as dwellings accrete, compute the perfusion field (hop-distance to the nearest lane), find the most under-served cell, sprout a capillary lane toward it; repeat until everything is within oxygenReach. The branching, space-filling street web of an organically grown town.
  • The grain of the fabric — Conzen (Alnwick, 1960): the plan accretes in three layers — streets → plots → buildings. Plots are burgage strips (long, narrow, perpendicular to the frontage), each living a burgage cycle: institutive → repletive (backland infills) → climax (saturation) → recessive (clearance to "urban fallow"). A per-plot state machine that ages density.
  • The first land-use gradient — von Thünen (1826): rent trades off against transport cost, R = Y(p−c) − Y·F·m, so each use bids a line in distance and land goes to the upper envelope. Perishable/heavy uses hug the centre; extensive uses push out.

→ rule grow lanes with the coverage solver; gate population by surplus; age each plot through the burgage cycle; assign land use by the bid-rent envelope on travel-distance to the core.

Phase 3 · the Physarum phaseNetworked maturation

The town becomes a city with trade, and the flow problem flips from coverage to demand.

  • Demand from gravity — Reilly (1931): interaction goes as T_ij ∝ P_i·P_j / d_ij² (inverse-square). This O–D matrix is the current we inject.
  • Arteries from flux — Tero et al., Science 2010 (the slime-mold Tokyo-rail result): route the demand, accumulate flux, and let each edge adapt, dD_ij/dt = f(|Q_ij|) − D_ij — thicken where used, decay where idle. Streets are the superlevel set. The one real knob is the feedback exponent μ: μ<1 keeps redundant parallel streets (a grid); μ>1 collapses everyone onto one arterial (a tree). μ is "planned vs organic."
  • Land use differentiates — Alonso (1964) sorts uses into commerce → industry → residential from the centre. Which pattern is a selectable kernel: concentric rings (Burgess, invasion–succession), sectors along transport rays (Hoyt), or multiple nuclei (Harris–Ullman: specialized facilities + agglomeration + repulsion + rent-affordability spawn several centres).
  • History freezes into the plan — Conzen's fringe belts: when growth pauses at a wall or common, low-density uses (cemeteries, barracks, parks) accrete there; when growth leaps past, that ring is embedded as a fossil of a former edge. The morphological frame (old walls, route lines, plot boundaries) constrains all later growth — what makes a generated city read as grown, not stamped.
  • The loop closes — Levinson & Xie co-evolution: new road → accessibility → development → demand → more road. polis iterates Phases 2–3 rather than running them once.

→ rule generate gravity demand between sub-centres; grow the road network with the flux solver (μ as the messiness dial); assign land use by the bid-rent envelope under a chosen structure kernel; drop fringe belts at each epoch edge; persist a morphological-frame mask; iterate.

Phase 4 · the regionThe system & the through-line

One city is never alone, and statistical regularities emerge across many.

  • Hierarchy — Christaller (1933): every service has a threshold (minimum market) and a range (max travel); a place's order is the highest service it supports. Market areas pack into hexagons; the nesting factor K picks the geometry — K=3 market, K=4 transport (lower centres fall on the arterials), K=7 administrative. Lösch loosens this into a per-good family of hexagons.
  • Size distribution — Zipf (1949) / Gibrat (1931) / Gabaix (1999): sizes follow P_n ≈ P_1/n. Why: Gibrat (growth rate independent of size) makes log-size a sum of shocks → lognormal; add a lower reflecting barrier (cities can't vanish) and the steady state is exactly Zipf (ζ=1). So we don't author the hierarchy — we run proportionate growth with a floor and it self-organizes.
  • Scaling — Bettencourt & West (PNAS 2007): Y = Y₀·N^β. Infrastructure sublinear (roads, cable; β≈0.85), socioeconomic output superlinear (GDP, patents, wages, and crime; β≈1.15), individual needs linear (housing, water; β≈1.0). Pick a city's N, then derive its road budget, wealth, crime and housing — no manual balancing, and the metropolis automatically feels denser and richer per capita.
  • The urban lifecycle — van den Berg's stages: urbanization → suburbanization → counter-urbanization → reurbanization, driven by falling transport cost — the macro clock the phases run under.

Path dependence — the through-line. Why early accidents persist (David 1985; Arthur 1989; Marshall 1890; Bleakley & Lin 2012). A founding advantage breaks the initial tie; increasing returns (labour pooling, supplier linkages, knowledge spillovers) amplify the lead via super-linear / preferential-attachment growth; and sunk, durable infrastructure does not decay when the founding feature dies. The clean proof: portage cities, founded at fall-line sites, kept concentrating population after river navigation became obsolete (~6% lower density per 10% farther from the dead portage). The lock-in test for polis: delete a city's founding feature mid-run — it should keep growing.

04How the pieces compose into one engine

Substrate (mappa). A seeded planet; per-cell water, rivers, relief, fertility, lithology.
Reaction–diffusion / site score. Place well-spaced nuclei (the Turing analogue) and rank sites by the two-axis site×situation score, with forced spawn points pre-seeded.
Scaling laws. Assign each nucleus a population N (sampled Zipf or grown by Gibrat-with-a-floor); derive its infrastructure budget N^0.85, output N^1.15, needs N^1.0.
Coverage solver (foam.js seize). While the town is small, grow the organic core by angiogenesis — keep the fringe within reach. Age plots through the burgage cycle.
Demand solver (flux.js growNetwork). As trade thickens, cross-fade to gravity demand → Physarum arteries (μ = grid↔tree). Sort land use by the bid-rent envelope; freeze fringe belts.
Co-evolution loop. New accessibility spawns development → new demand → more network. Iterate; lock-in keeps the accidents. Across many cities, the central-place hierarchy and Zipf line emerge.

Each step is a separate, swappable module — and the load-bearing two (coverage, demand) already exist, pure and node-tested, in hoop.

05Determinism, and the road to a 4X

Everything is seeded. (worldSeed, siteCell) → a city's entire history, identical on every machine, freezable to an ATProto record — exactly mappa's and hoop's contract. No Date.now(), no unseeded Math.random() in generation. A city permalink (and, later, a 4X save) is only meaningful if the history reproduces.

polis is the map-and-history layer a 4X needs. Once a city has a legible, sourced developmental history — a founding reason, an organic core, arteries that thickened with trade, quarters that differentiated, a place in a Zipf hierarchy — the 4X verbs have something real to act on: eXplore the mappa world, eXpand along the site-score field, eXploit the surplus/scaling economy, eXterminate over the central-place catchments. The phases above are, deliberately, also the era progression of such a game.

Next passes (as the theory hardens into code): a substrate.js mappa-reader; a site.js two-axis scorer with the forced spawn points; a vendored coverage.js (from foam.js) and network.js (from flux.js), re-synced not forked; and a deterministic history.js that drives the cross-fade and emits a replayable chronicle.
