A meander is a single closed loop crossing a horizontal line at 2n points, never touching itself. Build one from two halves: a non-crossing arch system above the line and another below. Superpose them — sometimes they fuse into one loop (a meander), sometimes they fall apart into several (a meandric system). Each row is a perfect matching of the 2n points by non-crossing arcs — there are Cn (a Catalan number) of them per row. The loop alternates: an arc above, an arc below, above, below. It's a meander exactly when that walk visits all 2n points in one cycle.

How many meanders of order n?

There are Cn non-crossing arch systems per row, so Cn² ways to stack a top and a bottom. The meandric number Mn counts just the stacks that form a single loop. The table below is computed live in your browser by brute force — generating every arch system, superposing every pair, and counting loops.

The sequence — 1, 2, 8, 42, 262, 1828, 13820, 110954, … (OEIS A005315) — has no known closed form. There is no formula, no generating function in closed form, no efficient way to compute Mn beyond direct enumeration. It is one of the cleanest unsolved counting problems in combinatorics: a schoolchild can state it, and nobody can solve it.

Notice how fast the meanders thin out against the Cn² pairs: most ways of stacking two arch systems fall into pieces. The growth tab asks how the survivors multiply.

Mn grows like C · R2n / nα. The ratio Mn+1/Mn climbs toward R² ≈ 12.26 — slowly. The exponent α is the strange part.

What a meander is

Draw an infinite horizontal line. Now draw a single closed curve — a loop — that crosses the line transversally at 2n points and never touches itself anywhere else. That's a closed meander of order n. Count the essentially different ones and you get the meandric numbers Mn: 1, 2, 8, 42, 262, 1828, 13820, 110954, 933458, …

The name is literal: the curve wanders back and forth across the line like the river Maíandros (modern Büyük Menderes) in Anatolia, whose looping course gave Greek its word for a winding path — and gave us the Greek-key border pattern.

The arch-system picture

Cut the plane along the line. Above it, the loop becomes a set of non-crossing arcs pairing up the 2n crossing points; below it, another. Each half is a non-crossing perfect matching of 2n points — and those are counted by the Catalan number Cn = (2n choose n)/(n+1). A meander is a pair (top matching, bottom matching) whose superposition is a single loop. So meanders live inside Cn² stacked pairs, and the entire difficulty is the word single: deciding when two independently-easy objects happen to interlock into one connected curve.

The pieces are Catalan-simple. The connectivity is the abyss.

Why it's hard — and the quantum-gravity exponent

Both the top and bottom matchings have a clean product formula (Catalan numbers). Their pairing does not. There is no known closed form for Mn, no nice generating function, no polynomial-time formula. The best exact tool is what the count tab does: enumerate.

What is known is the asymptotics. Mn ~ C·R2n/nα with a connectivity constant R ≈ 3.5018 and a configuration exponent

α = (29 + √145) / 12 ≈ 3.4201.

Di Francesco, Golinelli and Guitter obtained α in 1997 by mapping meanders to a gas of self-avoiding loops on a random surface — a c = −4 conformal field theory coupled to 2D quantum gravity. The irrational exponent with a √145 in it is the gravitational dressing (KPZ) of a conformal weight. It is genuinely startling that a question about doodling a loop over a line should have its growth rate dictated by a theory of fluctuating spacetime.

The determinant that does have a formula

Here is the sting. Arrange all Cn arch systems and form the meander matrix: entry (p, q) is q(number of loops when arch systems p and q are superposed). This is the Gram matrix of the Temperley–Lieb algebra's natural bilinear form. Di Francesco proved its determinant factors completely into a product of Chebyshev-type (cyclotomic-flavoured) polynomials in q — an exact, closed, beautiful product. Same objects, opposite epistemics: the number of single-loop meanders is hopeless, yet a natural determinant over the very same arch systems is one of the prettiest closed forms in the subject.

Meanders as factorizations

Label the crossings 1…2n along the line. The top matching and bottom matching are each fixed-point-free involutions of those labels. Following the loop is exactly composing the two involutions, and the configuration is a meander iff that composition is a single 2n-cycle. So counting meanders is counting factorizations of a long cycle into two non-crossing involutions — a question that lands squarely in the combinatorics of the symmetric group, map enumeration, and Hurwitz theory.

Folding stamps

Meanders are in bijection with ways to fold a strip of stamps flat. View a flat-folded strip edge-on: consecutive stamps are joined by creases that arch alternately over and under the stack — an arch system above and below a line. The single connected strip forces a single thread, a semi-meander; closing both ends gives a meander. The stamp-folding sequences (OEIS A000136, A000682) are cousins of Mn and just as resistant to a formula. The fold tab animates the correspondence.

History

  • 1912 — Henri Poincaré, working on the qualitative theory of differential equations and curves on surfaces, runs into the configurations now called meanders.
  • 1960s–70s — Touchard and Lunnon study the equivalent stamp-folding problem; the sequences enter the early tables of combinatorics.
  • 1980s — Arnold and the Russian school connect meanders to the topology of plane curves, Gauss codes, and singularity theory.
  • 1997 — Di Francesco, Golinelli & Guitter give the matrix-model / quantum-gravity analysis: the connectivity constant R and the exponent α = (29+√145)/12. Di Francesco proves the meander determinant product formula.
  • today — Mn is known only by enumeration into the low double-digit orders. The exact constant R, a closed form, and even whether Mn is "P-recursive" remain open.

Why this sits in the geometry pack

The pack collects extremal and combinatorial problems with a small-integer heart and a long, open climb — the lonely runner, Heilbronn's triangle. Meanders are the purest of the lot: the statement fits on a napkin, the answer is a sequence of integers, and the growth rate had to be borrowed from quantum gravity because no one can derive it by counting.
