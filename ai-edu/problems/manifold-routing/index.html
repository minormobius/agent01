Manifold routing: a Steiner tree with components attached

One inlet, a handful of outlets, a bonded plate. The shortest channel network that wires them all together is a rectilinear Steiner tree — and the wye junctions are its Steiner points. Drop a valve onto a leg and the pressure budget responds; pick a pump and you find out which leg busts it.

1. The brief

A bonded plate manifold has to connect one supply inlet to N outlets through internal channels, with a few valves on the legs you need to switch and sensors where you actually need to measure. The pump is fixed; the manifold has to fit on the plate; every outlet has to receive its share of flow; the worst-case path's pressure drop has to stay under the pump's head with margin. Two coupled problems: a topology (where do the channels run, where do they branch) and a sizing (which components, on which legs, in what order).

2. Why this is an afternoon, not a week

The topology is a classic rectilinear Steiner tree — one inlet, N sinks, minimise total channel length — and a small heuristic (Hanan grid + 1-Steiner) solves it in milliseconds for a handful of ports. The sizing is conservation plus Darcy–Weisbach plus the catalog's K-factors and Cv values. Claude can wire all of that into a live board for you. What it can't do is name the right pump curve, the right valve Cv at duty, and whether the deepest leg actually carries the flow it's spec'd for — those decisions are yours.

3. The board

Drag ports along the edges. Click Add outlet to grow the manifold; click an outlet to remove it. Pick a tool and click a channel segment to drop a valve or a sensor on it; click again to remove. The router re-solves on every change. The critical leg lights up red.

The router is doing two things at once. The skeleton is a Hanan–grid Steiner heuristic: candidate junction points sit at every (xi, yj) of the terminals, and one gets added if it shortens the spanning tree. The cost on each leg is the Darcy–Weisbach friction integrated along its length plus the minor losses you've placed: one K = 1 per elbow, one K = 0.5 at every wye it crosses, and (Q/Cv)2 for every valve on it. The worst-of-N outlet sums is the budget the pump has to clear.

4. Verification — the budget reconciles

No deadline like a CPM, but the same family of oracle. The schedule has the critical path; the manifold has the critical leg, and the pump must clear it.

Rung 1 — continuity at every wye. Flow in equals sum of flows out at every Steiner point. (With equal-share outlets this is automatic; with metered branches you balance restrictors against it.)
Pass: every junction reconciles. If it doesn't, you've added a leak or miscounted a branch.
Rung 2 — connectivity. Every outlet reaches the inlet.
Pass: the Steiner tree spans all terminals. A disconnected outlet is the router telling you the topology is wrong.
Rung 3 — the pump clears the worst leg. ΣΔP along the critical path ≤ pump head, with margin.
Pass: the worst-leg total is comfortably under the selected pump's head. The leg is highlighted red on the board so you know which one to fix.
Fail here: you can't move the answer with the chart; either shrink restrictions on the worst leg (bigger Cv valves, fewer bends) or step up the pump.
Rung 4 — manufacturability. Min bend radius, valve footprint clearance, plate envelope — all hard yes/no.
Pass: bend count and component clearances are within the plate's geometry budget; the topology is shipable as drawn.

6. Hints

Hint 1 — pick the pump first

The pump's pressure–flow curve is the budget the whole board lives inside. Sizing valves and channels before knowing the head you have to work with is how you end up routing a manifold the pump can't actually drive.

Hint 2 — the worst leg isn't always the longest

It's the highest sum: length × friction + bends + valve Cv terms. A short leg with a tight valve will eat more head than a long leg with a wide one. Watch the readout, not the picture.

Hint 3 — wyes have a cost too

Every Steiner junction adds a small minor loss to each branch passing through it (K_wye ≈ 0.5). A tree that minimises length isn't necessarily the one that minimises worst-case ΔP; some manifolds are better off with one extra meter of channel and one fewer junction.

Hint 4 — what to ask for
PromptGiven an inlet, N outlets, a plate envelope, and a pump curve (Q_max, ΔP_max): solve a rectilinear Steiner tree for the topology, compute Darcy–Weisbach + minor losses (one K=1 elbow per L-bend, K=0.5 per wye, (Q/Cv)² per valve) along each inlet→outlet leg, return the worst-leg ΔP and flag whether the pump head clears it with 20% margin. Recommend which leg to widen, which valve to swap up in Cv, or whether the pump itself is the wrong tool.

7. Where to draw the line

Let Claude run the Steiner heuristic, the friction integrals, the K-sum on every leg — tireless arithmetic on a network is exactly what it's good at. But you pick the pump curve, the valve at duty, and whether “within margin” means 20% or 50% for this fluid and this application. The math tells you which leg busts. Only you can tell it the budget is honest in the first place.

8. One worked solution

What good looks like

On the defaults — KNF NF 1.5 (60 mL/min, ~87 psi), 4 outlets, 80 mL/min total (clamped to pump max), 2 mm channels — the Steiner topology runs a single trunk across the plate and branches at the outlet edge (the wye sits on one of the outlets, which is what an honest Steiner heuristic does when terminals are collinear). Total channel length is in the dozens of mm; the worst-leg ΔP sits well under the pump head with margin. Drop a Lee LHD on one branch (tiny Cv) and that leg's ΔP jumps several psi — on the chart you can see why people use big-Cv Clippards for switching and Lee restrictors for shaping flow, not the other way around. Switch the pump to the TCS M200S-V and the budget rung fails immediately: lots of flow, no head — the wrong tool for a head-loss-dominated plate.

9. Going further

  • Multi-layer plate (vias): add a second routing layer with vertical connections, and turn the router loose — crossings stop being a constraint, total length usually drops.
  • Mass-flow control: add a per-branch metered restrictor (a Lee Lohm restrictor or an MFC) and balance the network so the outlets receive their specified shares, not equal shares.
  • Compressible flow: swap the working fluid to a gas and the friction equation switches to the compressible form — valves with their published Kv/Cv for air at standard conditions.
  • Cost as a co-objective: length × $/m + Σ component price; the cheapest manifold isn't the shortest one.
