the question

One hex packs 14 threads — 6 white + 8 production. Honeycomb it to make the world infinite. What happens to a thread where tile A's edge meets tile B's?

the solve

White = continuity. The hex has 6 edges; the cortex has 6 whites. Each white's rim-most cell claims one edge — an exact 1-white-per-edge bijection. Translation-tiling turns each owned white into a straight global strand, so the 6 whites collapse to 3 families: one azimuthal ring + two counter-rotating helices (the emergence, over the real Voronoi weave).

Production = K-doors. 8 engines don't divide the hex's 6-fold symmetry, so they can't all splice; they stay local and, where they graze the rim, abut the neighbour's other-kind thread — a cross-kind K-door. That is the K(6,8) contact reaching across the seam.

Continuity here is realised the way it is everywhere in rind: not literal curve-joining but door-adjacency between the rim cells that abut across the seam. Position along the edge decides which cell meets which.

weave the chunks — like with like

Each white spirals from the central hub to one rim edge, so a continuous warp thread is hub → seam → neighbour-hub. Translation-tiling leaves every white biased to the same side of its edge, so opposite edges don't line up. Reorienting each neighbouring chunk by a hexagon symmetry (rotate + mirror — the plain-weave over/under, made spatial) lands its spirals on top of the centre's at the seam, like with like: the families thread continuously across the web. The seam-mismatch readout drops when weave is on.
