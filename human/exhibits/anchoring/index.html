The museum plaque

When people estimate an unknown quantity, they start from whatever number is lying around — even a number they just watched a roulette wheel produce — and adjust from there. The adjustment is never enough. In Tversky and Kahneman's original 1974 demonstration, a wheel rigged to land on 10 or 65 preceded this exact question about African states in the UN: the group that saw 10 gave a median answer of 25%; the group that saw 65 gave 45%. A meaningless number, witnessed for three seconds, moved estimates by twenty points.

Anchoring has since been replicated across dozens of labs (including the 36-lab Many Labs project), with judges, doctors, and real-estate agents all proving as movable as undergraduates. Knowing about it helps less than you'd hope. (The true answer, as of the mid-2020s: 54 of 193 member states — about 28%.)
