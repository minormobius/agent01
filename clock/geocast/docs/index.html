How to read the geomancer's table

You make sixteen marks in the sand. From them the table raises a shield of figures and reads it. Here is what you are looking at, how to cast, and how to read.

What am I looking at?

Geomancy (ʿilm al-raml, “the science of the sand”) asks a question by making rows of marks without counting, and reading each row as odd or even. Four rows make a figure; sixteen rows make four figures — the Mothers — and from the Mothers the rest of the shield is generated by fixed rules. Nothing here is random in the machine: you are the randomness, in the sand.

The table never counts for you. It measures the marks you leave between each pair of parentheses and reports the parity. The reading is then composed from a real source — Robert Fludd’s Tractatus de Geomantia (1704) — every line traceable to the Latin on read.mino.mobi.

How to cast

Fresh sand
Sixteen lines wait, each between a pair of ( brackets ), in four groups of four — one group per Mother.
↓
Stab the dots — don’t count
Tap or drag dots along each line. Hold your question in mind; let the hand fall as it will.
↓
The table reads each line
It counts the distinct dots between the brackets: odd → • (single, active), even → •• (double, passive). You see the count and parity appear at each line.
↓
Read the cast ▸
When the marks are set, submit. Nothing is judged until you do. The cast then locks — the sand is untouchable until you clear it.
↓
⟲ Fresh sand
Clears everything back to a blank slate for a new question.

How the shield is built

Your sixteen lines are the four Mothers. Everything else follows by rule — no new input. The shield is read right → left (its Arabic origin), and narrows from eight figures to one:

  • Mothers (1–4) — your four cast figures, the seed of everything.
  • Daughters (5–8) — the Mothers transposed: the first line of each Mother becomes the first Daughter, and so on.
  • Nieces (9–12) — each made by adding a pair above it (line by line: two singles or two doubles → double; one of each → single).
  • Witnesses — the Right from the Mothers’ side (the querent), the Left from the Daughters’ (the matter).
  • Judge — the two Witnesses added: the verdict over the whole question. It always carries an even number of points.

How to read

Tap any place in the shield
Each figure in its place is read in that place’s light — what the figure means, here. Houses 1–12 are the provinces of the question (life, wealth, kin, home, … secret foes).
↓
Ask a question — pick the matter’s house
Choose the house your question belongs to (marriage → the 7th, money → the 2nd, and so on).
↓
Does it perfect?
The table links your significator (the figure in the 1st house) to the matter’s, by Fludd’s four connections — occupation, conjunction, mutation, translation. If they meet, the matter comes to pass.
↓
The aspect
How the two houses regard each other — trine & sextile favour it, square & opposition cross it.
The Judge
The temper of the end — good, ill, or mixed — and the two Witnesses read together.
↓
The judgment
Significators → the connection that joins them → the aspect that colours it → the Judge’s verdict. Every line links to the Latin on read.

A small glossary

Figure
Four lines, each single (•) or double (••) — one of sixteen. Top to bottom: Fire, Air, Water, Earth.
House
One of twelve provinces a figure can fall into — life, substance, kin, home, children, sickness, the other party, death, journeys, honour, friends, sorrows.
Witnesses & Judge
The right Witness is the querent’s side, the left the matter’s; together they make the Judge, the verdict.
Perfection
Whether the question is “brought to pass” — by the querent’s and the matter’s significators connecting (occupation, conjunction, mutation, or translation).
Aspect
How two houses regard each other by their distance — conjunction, sextile, square, trine, opposition.
Reconciler
An optional sixteenth figure (Judge + first Mother), a final confirmation; not shown on the table by default.
