What you're turning

This is the same rotor the brass tablet ships — a free wheel with sixteen sprung detents, one seat per figure — only here its constants are sliders. Each frame the angular acceleration is

α  =  −D·sin(16θ)  −  f·ω  −  g·sin(θ − θ★)

where D is detent bite, f is friction, and g is tilt toward the favoured seat θ★. Flick gives it an initial ω; it coasts, ratcheting notch by notch, until its momentum is spent and it drops into whichever well it last passed. With g = 0 and a healthy spin the landing well is anyone's guess.

Why the meter matters

A divining instrument must be fair: every figure equally likely. The surprise this meter delivers is how robust the honest knobs are. Because each cast begins wherever the last one happened to stop — an arbitrary seat — spin power, friction, and detent bite barely move the distribution: it stays flat and fairness sits near 100% across their whole range. What a weak spin really costs is independence — a barely-travelled cast lands close to the previous one, an autocorrelation the marginal histogram can't see.

The one knob that genuinely loads the wheel is tilt. A bench off level pours probability into the favoured seat; run the meter and the histogram spikes (its bar turns red), fairness collapsing toward the rigged figure. Honest mechanism, dishonest table. The lesson the tablet keeps quiet about — try to break it, and notice which knob actually can.
