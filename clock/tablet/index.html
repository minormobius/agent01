What this is

The only known medieval geomantic machine: a copper-alloy plate inlaid with gold and silver, made by al-Mawṣilī in 1241/42, now in the British Museum. The diviner turned dials whose windows revealed configurations of silver points — a randomiser in brass, the uncounted hand.

How the dial settles. Each disc here is simulated as a real rotor with sixteen detents — notched seats round the rim, one per figure. Flick it (tap, or grab and throw with the cursor) and it spins free, coasting under friction while the detents ratchet it notch by notch; when its momentum is spent it drops into whichever seat it last passed — and that well is the figure. Nothing is pre-chosen: the figure is wherever the physics comes to rest, exactly as a sprung brass detent would have behaved on the bench in Mosul.
