balance lab — a standing, self-righting quadruped

The myology lab grew muscles that hold a pose; this asks the harder question — can the skeleton stay up on its own against gravity, in real time? It runs a balance controller (Virtual Model Control, the method real legged robots use): treat the trunk as one rigid body, decide the wrench — force + torque — it needs to hold its height and stay level, then solve for the ground-reaction forces the planted feet must push with to produce it (least-squares, clamped to a friction cone). Shove it and it recovers from the state error; turn the legs off and only gravity remains, so it drops. Standing & push-recovery are solid; walking is the live edge — slow gaits hold, fast ones still tip.

What you're watching. The thin grey figure is the literal skeleton (from comparative-osteology data), rigidly carried by the controlled trunk. The thick struts are the legs, posed by two-link inverse kinematics from each hip down to where the controller decides the foot should be. Green arrows are the ground-reaction forces the balance solve asked each planted foot for — note they always sum to hold the body's weight and cancel its tipping torque. The whole thing is deterministic from the iNaturalist taxon id, so a given creature balances the same way on every machine. Walking adds a 4-beat gait schedule with Raibert foot placement; it's honest about its limits — push the speed up and you'll watch a fast walk lose its pitch (capture-point control is the next layer).
