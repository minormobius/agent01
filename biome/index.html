mino.mobi / biome

biome · a living O'Neill cylinder

tooling for thinking about the interior of an infinite (no end-caps) O'Neill cylinder run as a bio-engine for life support

A linear sun on the spin axis. Vegetation on the inner surface of the structural Voronoi rind. The enclosed air, water and soil doing the work of a closed life-support loop — oxygen, calories, water and nitrogen cycling in a sealed tube with no way out. This is the toolkit for working out whether that loop closes, where the water goes, and what the inside actually looks like.

The premise — why the inside is strange

Gravity is a gradient

Spin gravity g(r)=ω²r is full at the rim and falls to zero at the axis. "Up" means "toward the centre", where you weigh nothing.

Up is hot, not cold

The sun is on the axis; the cold sink is the shell radiating to space at the rim. That inverts Earth — and means warm air rises to the core and stays there.

Fog, not rain

Too little adiabatic range (~16 K across the whole radius) to build rain clouds. Condensation happens on the cold surface: a cloud-forest of dew and canopy fog.

The loop must close

No resupply. Photosynthesis, crew metabolism and soil microbes have to balance — and the stagnant air that waters the plants also starves them of CO₂.

Modules

Built in dependency order: close the books first, then resolve them in space, then render them.

1 · Resource cycles

live

A deterministic, element-exact box model of the closed ecology — O₂, CO₂, water, nitrogen and biomass as stocks and flows. Answers the zeroth question: does the loop close, how big must the buffers be, and where does the nitrogen go. Drag a knob and a model-year reruns live.

→ surfaces the real lessons: air closes easily but calories are hard · CO₂ self-limits as you add crop · oxygen security is banked carbon · the Biosphere-2 crash is a slider

2 · Radial atmosphere

planned

A 1-D column model along the radius: temperature, pressure, humidity and CO₂ as functions of altitude and time. Where "up is hot" stops being a slogan and becomes a fog-height number — the dew profile, the stratified dead-zone, and whether pulsing the sun ventilates the canopy. Takes its surface boundary from Module 1.

3 · Interior visualiser

planned

The iconic O'Neill view — looking "up" and seeing land curve overhead — but with the atmosphere rendered: the axial sun, the canopy on the inner surface, fog banding by altitude, and a WebGPU slice of the Coriolis-organised convection. Fed by Modules 1 and 2, so it shows validated state, not assumptions.
