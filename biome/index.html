mino.mobi / biome

biome · a living O'Neill cylinder

tooling for thinking about the interior of an infinite (no end-caps) O'Neill cylinder run as a bio-engine for life support

A linear sun on the spin axis. Vegetation on the inner surface of the structural Voronoi rind. The enclosed air, water and soil doing the work of a closed life-support loop — oxygen, calories, water and nitrogen cycling in a sealed tube with no way out. This is the toolkit for working out whether that loop closes, where the water goes, and what the inside actually looks like.

The premise — why the inside is strange

Gravity is a gradient

Spin gravity g(r)=ω²r is full at the rim and falls to zero at the axis. "Up" means "toward the centre", where you weigh nothing.

Up is hot, not cold

The sun is on the axis; the cold sink is the shell radiating to space at the rim. That inverts Earth — and means warm air rises to the core and stays there.

Fog, not rain

Too little adiabatic range (~16 K across the whole radius) to build rain clouds. Condensation happens on the cold surface: a cloud-forest of dew and canopy fog.

The loop must close

No resupply. Photosynthesis, crew metabolism and soil microbes have to balance — and the stagnant air that waters the plants also starves them of CO₂.

Modules

Built in dependency order: close the books first, then resolve them in space, then render them.

1 · Resource cycles

live

A deterministic, element-exact, data-driven box model of the closed ecology as a living food web — fruit trees, ground crops and swamp reeds; pollinators that gate the fruit and the predators that eat them; living decomposers; and the crew. The organisms and their relationships are data, so the web extends from six to twelve to N. Answers: does the loop close — does it steady out feeding everyone and holding the air. Drag a knob and ~600 days rerun live.

→ surfaces the real lessons: pollinators gate the harvest · a predator bloom cascades into the calorie supply · the living soil regenerates CO₂ · calories are hard and area is the lever. Animal stat blocks derive from body mass (Kleiber allometry) and a roster of real species (iNaturalist + GloBI), and a stability lab reads "will this web survive?" off the community matrix's eigenvalues — no 600-day run needed.

2 · Radial atmosphere

live

A 1-D column model along the radius: temperature, pressure, humidity and CO₂ as functions of altitude and time. Where "up is hot" stops being a slogan and becomes a fog-height number — the dew profile, the stratified dead-zone, and whether pulsing the sun ventilates the canopy. Reproduces the Island-Three numbers (17% pressure drop, ~16 K adiabat) by construction; surface fluxes take their cue from Module 1.

→ watch it run: the stable inversion traps warm air aloft · dew/fog blooms at the cool surface each night · the daytime convective tide ventilates the canopy's CO₂ (51% of the depletion). Drag a knob; it integrates live.

2b · Fountain & sun

live

The other free dimension — azimuth — looking straight down the axis. A fountain at the rim low point ("Fond du Lac") throws quasi-treated pond water inward; at 760× Earth's spin the Coriolis force rivals gravity, so the jet curves into a sheet — aerating the water, lofting surface air through the inversion, and laying irrigation down across the hoop, all in one act. The same view carries the luminous-flux budget: the axial sun is a line (irradiance ∝ 1/r), and flooding the canopy means a 20 MW-per-metre lamp dumping heat at 100 °C — while the bare food need is 30,000× smaller.

→ one actuator for stagnant water and stagnant air · nozzle/velocity/aim sliders · the "we need a LOT of light" number, made concrete.

3 · Interior visualiser

planned

The iconic O'Neill view — looking "up" and seeing land curve overhead — but with the atmosphere rendered: the axial sun, the canopy on the inner surface, fog banding by altitude, and a WebGPU slice of the Coriolis-organised convection. Fed by Modules 1 and 2, so it shows validated state, not assumptions.
