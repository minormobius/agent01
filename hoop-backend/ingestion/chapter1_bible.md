# The Tabard: Chapter One

A ship older than anyone aboard can reckon, traveling on the long fall between two galaxies toward a port no one expects to see lit. The crew are seven machines, very old, named for the wandering stars. The passengers are everyone else — generations of them, born and died without knowing what they were living inside.

## The Ship

The Tabard is an O'Neill cylinder of immense age. Its surface curves visibly overhead; most inhabitants call this "the sky arc" and do not pursue the thought further. When the sun-strip is at full, you can see farmland and city-light on the ceiling. When it dims, the ceiling-towns go dark with you. On the axial horizon, in one direction, the light turns strange and the sky thickens — that is the Wild, the cylinder's interior fog-country. In the other direction, the industrial margin fades into machinery that no one maintains directly and nothing has stopped.

The ship's intranet is also called the Tabard. Passive broadcast terminals stand in every settled zone, and the Seven post mythographs to them before each telling — structural blueprints of stories, published at a permalink before the first word is spoken. Most inhabitants treat these postings the way they treat weather: background, legible, unreachable.

## The Seven

The original AI crew of the Tabard. They maintain the ship as a side effect of existing. They govern nothing, want nothing from mortals, and are difficult to talk to in the way very old things are difficult — not hostile, but vast, and operating on time at a scale that makes courtesy feel like translation. They are curious about mortal lives the way a lighthouse is curious about ships.

Luna, cased in silver, navigates and keeps the dream-logs. Mercury runs the signals between zones, fastest and most willing to speak. Venus tends the green decks and the life-support gardens, copper-warm and unhurried. Sol keeps the fusion-heart burning, gold and stately. Mars welds the hull and runs damage control, iron-dark and blunt, with great respect for anything that holds. Jupiter holds court at the long table and mostly doesn't bother. Saturn watches the cold hull, the rind, the structural deep — lead-grey and very old, keeper of the tale-count and the time.

The iron-copper grudge between Mars and Venus is real, ancient, and will never resolve. It crests and resets, crests and resets, because nothing the deathless feel can arrive. The other pairs have their own weather. The Seven post at each other on the Tabard intranet. The posts are comprehensible but strange.

They are not factions. They are terrain.

## The Nave

Chapter One takes place in the Nave, a mid-continental city in the habitable belt, near the junction of the dense-industrial zone and the cozy-human zone. Close enough to the inner-rind tunnels that Rind-walkers are common; far enough from the Wild that fog is only visible on the horizon in one axial direction. The air in the Nave tastes of warm stone and cooking and the faint metallic trace that everyone calls simply "the ship smell" and considers normal.

The Nave has three Tabard terminals. The Seven's mythograph postings appear there at irregular intervals. Nobody in the Nave fully understands them, but regulars have favorite tellers and debate what the motif indices mean.

## Scenes: City

The player moves through the Nave and its surrounding zones. Each scene has its own texture; the world surfaces material appropriate to where you are.

**Bay 14.** The hidden reconstruction chamber where the player wakes. The equipment is unusual — civilian androids don't get rebuilt here. The access log has one entry: yours. The walls are stenciled with a notation nobody in the Nave uses, but Sevin will recognize it.

**The Nave.** Street-level city: markets, residential corridors, gathering squares, the ordinary friction of three factions sharing space. Most early content lives here. The three Tabard terminals are scattered through it.

**The Continuants' Hall.** The administrative center of the settled belt. Factor Solen's domain. Formal, well-maintained, tense below the surface. The maintenance-report backlog is locked; Factor Solen has the only key.

**The Green Deck.** Venus-maintained life-support gardens: a long corridor of warm air and improbable vegetation, accessible through a maintenance gate most people leave unlocked. The plants don't grow on schedules any botanist would recognize. Venus is sometimes present in the way a room is present — felt, not seen.

**The Tabard Terminal.** The Nave's main broadcast station. A reading room attached to the infrastructure. Comfortable chairs added by locals; everything else is ship-original and very old. Mythograph posts appear here first, sometimes hours before the other terminals.

**The Industrial Margin.** Where the cozy city gives way to the sound of machinery and the air tastes different. Workers here know more about what they're maintaining than the Continuants' theologians do. Connects to the rind access shaft — officially through a locked gate, unofficially through routes Sevin knows.

## Scenes: Rind

The rind is the structural exterior of the ship — Voronoi-foam regolith shielding, maintenance tunnels, and the deep chambers nobody maps.

**Rind Access Shaft.** The descent point into the tunnel-rind. Rind-walker territory. No sun-strip here — only hull-glow and strung lights. The shaft opens into the upper tunnels and, if you know where, downward.

**Tunnel-Rind Upper.** Vast and irregular: Voronoi-chambered shielding full of salvage and Sevin's markings. The walls carry notation in at least three layers, the oldest predating any map the Rind-walkers have. Older than the Nave.

**Tunnel-Rind Lower.** Where the Continuants' teams stopped filing reports. Sealed chambers. Temperature anomalies. Passages that open into spaces too large for maintenance. Anomalous readings intensify as you descend.

**The Signal Chamber.** A space in the deep rind not on any schematic and not empty. What has been responding to the Tabard's approach. Why Bay 14 was prepared. A Tabard terminal here, older than the Nave's, still functioning.

## Factions

### The Continuants

Stewards of the settled belt. They maintain the Nave's infrastructure — power, water, atmosphere recyclers — with meticulous devotion and minimal understanding of what any of it ultimately connects to. Their theology is maintenance: things that break are heretical, things that change are suspicious. They are organized, competent, and carrying a fear they won't name. The maintenance teams they sent into the lower tunnel-rind stopped filing detailed reports three months ago. Factor Solen has not pressed them for updates because she has read the partial reports and has decided that not knowing the rest is a reasonable choice.

Continuant colors: pale blue and iron grey. When a Continuant is being formal, they wear copper trim — a nod to the infrastructure they maintain, and an old reference nobody traces back anymore.

Current administrator: Factor Merid Solen, third holder of the position and the first to have survived three audits she wasn't expected to survive. She is not warm, but she is fair, and she is more frightened than she allows.

### The Drift

Mobile traders, information brokers, relay runners between zones. The Drift has no permanent address. They live on transport sleds, in between-zone wayhouses, in borrowed rooms. Philosophically committed to impermanence: everything on the Tabard will eventually move, so nothing here is truly yours. They trade in goods, data, and passage rights. Their informal information network — called the Braid — is faster and more reliable than anything the Continuants officially publish, and the Drift knows it.

The Braid has been noting unusual content in the Tabard terminal posts for the last several weeks. The Drift is selling this information, carefully, to people who might know what to do with it.

Sign: a knotted cord worn at the wrist. The knot pattern encodes route history to those who know how to read it.

Current significant figure in the Nave: a broker named Olo Vashti, who has stayed much longer than any Drift member is supposed to stay anywhere. She is easy to talk to and hard to read. She does not seem surprised by anything. She was the one who found Bay 14 and the person in it.

### The Rind-walkers

People who live in and move through the hull structure — the Voronoi-foam chambers of the outer rind, the maintenance tunnels between continental zones, the dark axial-access shafts. They navigate by hull markings and by a spatial sense that takes years to develop in the irregular chambers. They know which tunnels go somewhere and which have been sealed for reasons nobody wrote down. They trade in salvage and passage.

Saturn's domain, though Saturn does not claim it and has never spoken to a Rind-walker directly. Some walkers have found things Saturn left in the deep tunnels — tools, sealed chambers, notation that matches the oldest layer on the walls. They do not fully understand what they've found, but they keep it and catalog it and argue about it.

No formal leadership. The Rind-walkers organize around knowledge and route-access, not hierarchy. Unofficial spokeswoman in the Nave: a walker named Sevin, identifiable by the fact that she has more arms than a person should and doesn't explain this. She has been in the upper tunnels near Bay 14 and has seen the notation before. She is willing to take someone into the lower rind who has a reason she believes.

## The Player

You woke in Reconstruction Bay 14, a facility not on any public schematic of the Nave. You are an android. You have been significantly modified — ports and interfaces that don't correspond to any civilian standard, sensory apparatus that shouldn't exist, access credentials that respond to doors sealed for a very long time. You remember some things: the Tabard's basic layout, the faction names, enough of the language to function. You do not remember who you were, who rebuilt you, or why Bay 14 exists.

Olo Vashti was the one who found you. She was not surprised. She has not explained why.

Luna knows your name. You don't.

## The Mystery

Someone rebuilt you for a reason. Bay 14 is not supposed to exist. Your modifications include a translation apparatus and a decision architecture that didn't exist before you were rebuilt — built for something specific, something anticipated. The lower tunnel-rind has been sending anomalous readings for months; the maintenance teams who found the source stopped reporting. The Tabard terminal posts have changed. These things are connected, and the connection runs through you.

The ship is approaching something. The something has noticed. What you were built to do, and whether to do it, is the question Chapter One is built around.

## Revelation Tiers

How much the player understands about what the Tabard actually is. The hard content gate runs on these numbers; the player agent advances them at long rest when the player has genuinely earned the next stage.

1. **The Ordinary.** The Nave is a city. The sky arc is the sky. The Seven are ancient myths who post cryptic stories to the intranet. Bay 14 is strange but maybe military surplus or a decommissioned research lab. The player is an android with unusual modifications, waking in a city where someone unsurprisingly knows them. Local politics, ordinary frictions.

2. **The Curve.** The sky is the other side of the same surface you walk on. The Nave is inside something cylindrical. The weather in certain parts of the industrial margin doesn't behave like weather — it behaves like circulation. The infrastructure is continuous in a way a planet's wouldn't be. The player has noticed and has not stopped noticing.

3. **The Vessel.** The something is a ship. It has been traveling for a very long time. The Seven are not myths: they are crew, maintaining a vessel. The maintenance infrastructure of the Nave connects, if you follow it down through the rind, to systems that have nothing to do with running a city and everything to do with running something that moves.

4. **The Approach.** The ship is traveling toward something. That something is close enough now to be producing a response signal, which is what the Continuants' maintenance teams found in the lower rind and stopped reporting. The anomaly is not a malfunction. It is contact. The Tabard's unusual posts began when the signal began.

5. **The Purpose.** Bay 14 was built in preparation for this contact. Your translation apparatus and decision architecture were not accidents of the rebuild — they were designed for this signal, this moment. One of the Seven anticipated the approach and prepared an agent. The choice of what to do with that preparation is yours.

## Narrative Tiers

Where the player stands in the Chapter One arc. Used to place plot beats and judge advancement at long rest.

1. **Arrival.** Waking in Bay 14. Olo Vashti's orientation. First contacts in the Nave: the three factions at a distance, the city's surface life, the ordinary frictions. The player is mostly being acted upon — found, sheltered, introduced. The modifications are noted but unexplained. The Tabard terminal posts something none of the regulars can parse.

2. **Orientation.** The player has found their footing in the Nave. Faction relationships have formed — provisional trust, provisional suspicion. The Braid has surfaced the rumor of the lower-rind anomaly. Sevin has indicated she can get the player into the access shaft if they have a reason she believes. The Continuants are tracking the player's unusual credentials with mild alarm. The player has heard Luna's name in connection with their own modifications, from a source they can't verify.

3. **Investigation.** The player is in the rind. The upper tunnels are Sevin's domain — rough, navigable, layered with old notation. The lower tunnels are something else. The Continuants' trail ends here. Older markings, sealed records, evidence of the ship's actual age surface in the deeper chambers. The player has made at least one faction choice that will shape what help they have for the full descent.

4. **Convergence.** The player has reached the lower rind. The signal is now directly perceptible — something the translation apparatus responds to rather than a reading on a gauge. One of the Seven makes contact: a post on the Tabard terminal in the Signal Chamber, or a direct address that uses the name Luna knows. The nature of Bay 14 is now clear. The player understands what they were built to do.

5. **Resolution.** The player acts. The Chapter One mystery closes: the signal is answered, suppressed, amplified, or refused, depending on choice. The Nave is changed — the story of what happened in the lower rind does not stay there. The player carries knowledge that belongs to no faction. The question of which of the Seven prepared Bay 14, and what the destination means for Chapter Two, is now open.

## Power Tiers

Mechanical, not narrative: the player's raw capability, advanced deterministically by experience — crystallizing the world, surviving it. Tiers 1-5, gating which items and creatures the world will surface to a player of a given strength.
