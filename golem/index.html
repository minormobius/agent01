Build a swarm; it decides what it is; it acts like it

This is the cube bench grown into a world. The bricks are the same: each living cube runs the exact ~26k-parameter neural cellular automaton lifted from the cube3D ESP32 firmware, holding a 28-channel state and gossiping with its 6 face-neighbours until every cube independently concludes what the whole body is — plane, chair, car, table, house, guitar, or boat. No cube ever sees the shape. The new part: you build the body, Minecraft-style, while it thinks out loud — and when you wake it, its identity becomes a verb.

The build loop

Tap a face to snap a cube onto it (tap the pad to start). The consensus bars run live while you build: place a slab and watch Table rise; add a backrest and Chair overtakes it; stretch the slab, lose the back, add stub wheels and it flips to Car. Mirror symmetry is on by default because vehicles are symmetric and thumbs are imprecise. Every build is a permalink — the whole 15³ structure is 563 characters of URL.

Actuation — identity as a verb, decentralised where it counts

When you wake a creature, three local mechanisms decide how it moves. None of them is a script with a global view:

  • throttle = conviction The body's speed is gated by the vote: the fraction of cubes agreeing on the lead class. A confused swarm barely creeps; near-unanimity drives at full speed. Smash pieces off mid-drive and it falters exactly as much as its classification does. Undecided cubes also fidget — twitch amplitude is belief entropy — so a fresh build visibly trembles, then steadies as consensus locks.
  • clock = Kuramoto The gait rhythm comes from a phase oscillator in every cube, coupled to its face-neighbours (plus the sway of the shared body — a mean-field term with a mechanical excuse). From random phases they synchronise in a couple of seconds into a shared clock no one owns. Bounce, waddle steps, bobbing, guitar plucks all ride this clock. Cut a body in half and each half's clock re-syncs independently.
  • verb = the lead class Cars drive (wandering, suspension bouncing on the clock). Planes taxi, climb, fly a banking circuit, land, repeat. Boats sail — water appears beneath them. Chairs and tables waddle, rocking on their legs in clock-step. Guitars go nowhere and pluck pentatonic notes on every clock beat. Houses refuse: one shiver, a settle of a few centimetres, chimney smoke. Identity ≠ mobility, and the house knows it.

Mitosis

Erase works on awake creatures too. Cut a car in half and the connected components become independent creatures: each keeps its cubes' beliefs (continuity of self — the states are carried over, not reset), runs its own gossip, syncs its own clock, and acts on its own verdict. One half may still believe Car and limp on; the other may decide it's a Chair now and waddle away. Fragments smaller than three cubes don't survive — they tumble off as debris.

Honesty notes

The classifier is the real, unmodified firmware network, and it was trained on ShapeNet voxelisations — it has never seen your build. It will happily be wrong, disagree with itself, or flip classes as you edit; the bars don't lie. Locomotion, by contrast, is theatre with decentralised controls: the body moves as one rigid thing (real modular cubes would pivot or slide lattice-wise — a much harder, slower dance), but whether, how fast, and to what rhythm are all read off per-cube local state. The docs on the cube bench cover the port's fidelity: parity with the original TF graph to <5×10⁻⁶, 99.4% per-cube accuracy over the repo's full dataset.

Engine

No build step, no physics engine, no WASM — plain ES modules over Float32Arrays, one THREE.InstancedMesh for every cube of every creature. The four modules are pure and node-tested (node golem/js/golem.selftest.mjs — 37 checks): nca.js (the firmware network + place/damage/extract), body.js (connected components, vote census, principal axis), gaits.js (Kuramoto field + the seven verbs, deterministic by seed), builder.js (symmetric edits, undo, the permalink codec).
