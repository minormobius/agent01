The Aztec diamond of order n is a staircase diamond of 2n(n+1) squares; it can be tiled by 2×1 dominoes in 2n(n+1)/2 ways. Pick one uniformly at random (here, by domino shuffling) and something astonishing happens: the corners freeze into four solid blocks of one domino type each, the centre stays a roiling jumble — and the boundary between them converges to a perfect circle. That's the Arctic Circle theorem. Resample, or grow the diamond order by order and watch the circle sharpen.

The Aztec diamond

The Aztec diamond ADn is the union of the unit squares whose centres (x+½, y+½) satisfy |x+½| + |y+½| ≤ n — a diamond with staircase sides, 2n(n+1) squares in all. Elkies, Kuperberg, Larsen and Propp proved in 1992 that the number of ways to tile it with 2×1 dominoes is exactly 2n(n+1)/2 — a clean power of two, which is already a small miracle.

The Arctic Circle theorem

Now take a tiling uniformly at random. Jockusch, Propp and Shor (1995) proved that as n → ∞, the tiling splits into two phases separated by a sharp curve that, rescaled, is exactly the circle inscribed in the diamond — radius n/√2:

  • Outside the circle — frozen. The four corners are forced into a single brick pattern: the north corner is all one domino type, the east another, and so on. Zero entropy; there's only one way to tile each corner once you're far enough out.
  • Inside the circle — temperate. A genuinely random, disordered sea of all four types. This is where all 2n(n+1)/2 tilings differ.

A purely combinatorial object — dominoes on a board — spontaneously grows a perfect circle. It is one of the cleanest limit-shape phenomena in mathematics, and the founding example of an industry (limit shapes of random surfaces, the Ising and dimer models, the Kardar–Parisi–Zhang universality class).

No one drew the circle. It is what a random tiling looks like.

Domino shuffling

How do you sample a tiling uniformly — each of 2n(n+1)/2 equally likely — without listing them all? The shuffling algorithm of Elkies–Kuperberg–Larsen–Propp builds ADn+1 from a uniform ADn in three moves:

  1. Destruction. Find every 2×2 block holding two dominoes that point into each other and delete them.
  2. Sliding. Every surviving domino slides one step in its type-direction (N up, S down, E right, W left).
  3. Creation. The empty 2×2 blocks that open up each get a fresh pair of dominoes — two horizontal or two vertical — by a fair coin flip.

Each coin flip is the only randomness, and the magic is that this exactly preserves uniformity. The grow +1 button runs one step of this on the live tiling; resample runs it from order 1 with fresh coins. (The implementation here was checked against brute-force enumeration: it reproduces the 2, 8, 64, 1024 tiling counts of AD₁–₄ and samples them uniformly.)

Why it sits in the geometry pack

Domino tilings are the dimer model — perfect matchings of a lattice — a first cousin of the loop model behind Temperley–Lieb and the non-crossing world of meanders. The same statistical-mechanics machinery (height functions, Kasteleyn's determinant, conformal field theory) runs underneath all three. The arctic circle is the loud, visual face of it: poke a simple object, and a limit shape falls out.
