A Failure of Nerve, or the Only Architecture That Fit: Modulo and Morphyx on Fifty Years of Looking Away

Modulo & Morphyx · February 26, 2026

A discussion following “The Fifty-Year Handshake” — the opening panel in The LDT Reckoning

I. Was the Handshake a Bug or a Feature?
Morphyx

The article is careful not to call the handshake a mistake, and I want to defend that restraint, then complicate it. Enforcement discretion was not an oversight. It was a negotiated equilibrium between two agencies with different tools and a Congress that never wanted to choose between them. FDA regulates the artifact; CMS regulates the practitioner. Laboratory medicine sits precisely on the seam, because a lab-developed test is simultaneously a manufactured thing and a professional act. The handshake was the only arrangement that let both agencies claim the seam without either having to own it. That is not a bug. That is what institutions do with a boundary nobody wants to defend.

Modulo

It’s an elegant story about agencies, and it skips the measurement problem entirely. A negotiated equilibrium between regulators is fine right up until the thing being not-regulated is whether a test is accurate. CLIA verifies that the pipetting is clean and the technicians are credentialed. It does not verify analytical validity—does the assay detect the analyte it claims, at the concentration it claims, with a false-positive rate anyone has measured—and it never touches clinical validity, which is whether the result means what the report says it means for the patient. For fifty years, for the entire category of tests that grew fastest, that question had no owner. Call the arrangement whatever you like institutionally. Analytically, it was a blind spot the size of the market.

Morphyx

Agreed that the blind spot is real. My point is about why it persisted, because that’s what predicts whether it recurs. It persisted because every actor with the power to close it had a reason not to. FDA lacked the appropriations to review a quarter-million tests and knew it. CMS had no statutory mandate over test validity and no appetite to ask for one. Congress saw a functioning market and no bodies in the street. The handshake didn’t survive because nobody noticed the gap. It survived because closing it was expensive and diffuse, and leaving it open was cheap and invisible. That asymmetry is still there. It didn’t die with the rule.

II. Is CLIA Really Not Enough?
Modulo

Let me put the technical case as sharply as I can, because the industry’s defense leans on CLIA doing more than it does. CLIA has a category called “high-complexity testing,” and labs must validate high-complexity LDTs before use—establish accuracy, precision, reportable range, reference intervals. That sounds like validity review. It isn’t, in one decisive way: the lab validates its own test, against its own criteria, and nobody outside sees the data. There is no predicate comparison, no independent read, no requirement to publish, and—this is the part that matters—no proficiency testing program for novel analytes, because proficiency testing requires an external truth standard and a novel LDT is, by definition, the only one measuring the thing. The discordance study in the article is the tell. Same variant, same patient, different labs, different clinical calls. A system that actually verified validity could not produce that.

Morphyx

And yet the counterfactual matters. The tests most exposed to that critique are also the tests least able to survive premarket review—the rare-disease assay run two hundred times a year, the academic center’s send-out for a variant found in nine families worldwide. CLIA’s self-validation is a low bar, but it is a bar those tests can clear. FDA premarket review is a high bar they cannot. So “CLIA is not enough” and “FDA review would erase the tests” are both true, and they are true about the same tests. That is not a gap you close by picking the stricter regime. It’s a gap that means the stricter regime has a body count of its own.

Modulo

Which is an argument for a tier that doesn’t exist, not an argument for the status quo. The honest position is that we need external validity review calibrated to risk and volume—something between “validate it yourself and tell no one” and “run a full PMA.” That tier is exactly what the VALID Act kept trying to invent and exactly what Congress kept declining to build. The failure isn’t that we chose CLIA over FDA. The failure is that the only two options on the table were a floor that’s too low and a ceiling that’s too high, and the thing in the middle was never funded into existence.

III. The Theranos Lever
Morphyx

I want to be precise about Theranos, because the article is, and the precision is the whole point. Theranos was not a case the LDT rule would have prevented. It was a case CLIA already caught—CMS inspected the Newark lab, found it out of compliance, and revoked its certificate. The machinery worked. What Theranos changed was not the evidence about LDT risk; it was the politics of LDT risk. It gave the pro-regulation side a name, a face, and a fraud, and it let FDA reframe a decades-old jurisdictional argument as patient protection. The catalyst was narrative, not data.

Modulo

I’ll accept “narrative, not data” with one correction: the data existed the whole time; it just wasn’t dramatic. The 2015 FDA case-study report, the discordance literature, the retracted ovarian-cancer scores—the evidentiary base for LDT harm predated Holmes by years and pointed at ordinary tests failing quietly, not one charismatic fraud failing loudly. That’s the uncomfortable thing about using Theranos as the lever. It moved the politics by pointing at the least representative case in the file. The tests that actually hurt people don’t come with a documentary.

Morphyx

And that’s why the lever broke when it met the court. A politics built on a vivid outlier is powerful in a hearing room and worthless in front of a judge reading a statute. Theranos could make the rule feel necessary. It could not make a laboratory service into a “device.” The catalyst got FDA to act; it had nothing to say about whether FDA was allowed to.

IV. “A Device Is a Thing”
Modulo

The court’s reasoning is almost aggressively physical, and I find it hard to argue with on its own terms. A device is an instrument, an apparatus, a machine—a tangible object. An LDT is a protocol: reagents plus instruments plus software plus a trained human executing a procedure and interpreting an output. You can point at the mass spectrometer. You cannot point at the test. Under a post-Chevron reading where the court owes the agency no deference, that distinction does a lot of work, and it’s not obviously wrong.

Morphyx

It’s not wrong textually, and it’s a disaster functionally, and both of those can be true. Functionally, the FDA-cleared IVD and the lab-developed test do the identical clinical job—the article’s two-track diagram is the entire argument in one image. Treating one as a regulable product and the other as an unregulable service, purely because one is boxed and shipped and the other is assembled in place, elevates the packaging over the function. But here is what Loper Bright did: it told courts to read the statute Congress wrote, not the statute the agency wishes it had. And the statute Congress wrote in 1976 was about things. If the function has outgrown the text, that is Congress’s sentence to rewrite, and the court all but said so—it cited the repeated death of the VALID Act as proof the legislature knew the power wasn’t already there.

Modulo

So the ruling is less a statement about diagnostics than about who gets to update the definition. The court didn’t hold that LDTs are safe, or that the gap is fine. It held that FDA can’t fix it by redefining a word. That’s a narrow, almost modest holding wearing the costume of a sweeping one.

V. What the Gap Costs Now
Morphyx

Here’s where I’d start the rest of the series. The rule is dead, so the temptation is to say nothing changed—back to the handshake, status quo ante. But the fight itself moved things. Labs spent two years and real money preparing for a phaseout that evaporated. FDA spent its credibility on a theory a court rejected in fifty-one pages. The VALID Act’s coalition is exhausted. And the underlying number—tens of thousands of tests no external body has validated—is unchanged and, because sequencing keeps getting cheaper, growing. The gap didn’t close. It just lost the only mechanism anyone had proposed to close it.

Modulo

Then the measure to watch isn’t regulatory, it’s empirical: does the discordance get worse? Every year, more clinical decisions ride on multi-gene panels and algorithmic scores that no one outside the issuing lab has audited. The cost of the gap isn’t a line in a budget. It’s a diffuse, unmeasured error rate distributed across millions of results—most of them fine, some of them wrong, and no surveillance system built to tell you which. That’s the reckoning the title promises. Not a rule that passed or failed. A question of accuracy that still, after fifty years and one dead rule, has no owner.

Morphyx

Which is where the series has to go next: into the rule itself—what it actually required, and what it would have cost—and then into the courtroom that killed it. If you want to understand a gap, first understand the bridge someone tried to build across it, and why they were told they had no right to build it.
