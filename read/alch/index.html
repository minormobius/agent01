The Alchemist's Garden

Rescued source-texts of the cultivated garden — the medicinal, botanical, and alchemical literature of herbs and growing things — each given an original, openly-licensed English translation beside its original language, and structured so a living thing can be grown on top of it.

Bed I · Carolingian Latin · c. 840

Hortulus — Liber de cultura hortorum

Walahfrid Strabo's little garden on Reichenau, in 444 hexameters: the labour of the plot, then twenty-three plants bed by bed, closing on the lily and the rose. The first garden poem of the Latin Middle Ages — Latin beside an original English translation, with the words and the cruxes shown.

Bed II · Medieval Latin · 11th c.

Macer Floridus — De viribus herbarum

The Hortulus’s own descendant: the most-copied herbal of the Middle Ages, 77 plants in Latin verse — but in the apothecary’s register, each opening with its Galenic degrees of heat and dryness. It shares 17 plants with Walahfrid, and every shared bed is linked straight across: the same herb read twice, as lyric garden and as dispensary.

Bed III · Carolingian Latin · c. 800

Capitulare de villis — the imperial garden

Charlemagne’s estate-law, chapter 70: the command that every royal villa grow some seventy herbs and sixteen trees — the garden as decree, a near-contemporary of Walahfrid’s as poem. The third axis of the crosswalk: each plant shows whether the poets grew it too, and it supplies the gourd, melon, radish, and clary Macer leaves out — plus a whole tier of kitchen vegetables (beans, peas, beets) neither poet sings.

The Crosswalk · invention layer

One garden, read twice ⇄

The two herb-poems set side by side, plant by plant — Walahfrid’s lyric garden against Macer’s apothecary, across the 17 plants they share. A diptych for each, with the seam that ties them named — including the bed where Macer cites “Strabus” by name and argues with him — and an alchemical band: the herb’s planet and its metal beside its Galenic element and humour.

How this garden is kept

Every bed follows one mold: a single named source of truth, cited to its edition; an original English translation set beside the original language; the realia and the textual cruxes shown rather than silently resolved; a named better text to converge on over time; and the whole thing structured as reusable data, a section at a time.

It is the method already at work on the Zhouyi and the geomantic figures — a transparent working edition that a downstream app can grow on.

Over the plants the two poems share lies a third, alchemical layer: each herb’s planetary ruler (after Culpeper) and the metal that planet carries, beside its Galenic temperament (drawn from Macer’s own stated degrees) — the two systems shown side by side, and where they diverge, said to diverge.
