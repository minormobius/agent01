One garden, read twice

Walahfrid Strabo’s Hortulus and Macer Floridus’s De viribus herbarum — two Latin herb-poems two centuries apart — bed against chapter, the lyric garden beside the apothecary.

Seventeen plants grow in both poems. Walahfrid (c. 840) writes a personal, lyric garden; Macer (11th c.) writes the clinical herbal that became the most-copied of the Middle Ages — every entry opening with its Galenic degrees of heat and dryness. Read side by side, the same herb turns twice. And in one bed the two poems are not merely parallel but in conversation: Macer’s lovage chapter names “Strabus” — Walahfrid Strabo himself — and argues with him.
