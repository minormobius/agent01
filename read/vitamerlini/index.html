Vita Merlini

Geoffrey of Monmouth's Life of Merlin — a Latin poem of 1,529 hexameters, written about 1150. Not the boy-prophet of the History but an older, stranger Merlin: a king and seer of the Welsh who runs mad with grief on the battlefield of Arfderydd, flees to the Caledonian Wood, and becomes the archetypal wild man — sung back to his wits, chained, set free by the threefold death, riding a stag to his wife's wedding, prophesying the kings to come, and hearing Taliesin tell of the Island of Apples where Morgen tends the wounded Arthur. The first Latin tale on this site, and the deepest taproot of the Merlin legend.

A deep read of the Vita Merlini (The Life of Merlin), Geoffrey of Monmouth's Latin poem of about 1150 — the first Latin text on this site, and the oldest literary taproot of the Merlin legend after Geoffrey's own History of the Kings of Britain. The Latin source is the text of John Jay Parry's edition (The Vita Merlini, University of Illinois, 1925, public domain); the original English translation runs alongside it movement by movement, with character cards, character web, story graph, desire, motif index, mythograph and a faithful storybook retelling — the same apparatus built for Sir Gawain and the Green Knight, Culhwch ac Olwen, Sir Orfeo, the Four Branches of the Mabinogi (Pwyll, Branwen, Manawydan, Math), and Owain. Parry's own 1925 English is consulted as a sense-check, not copied; both source texts are preserved verbatim under source/. This site sits alongside the broader Arthurian and Brittonic historiography at read.mino.mobi/pendragon, where the cross-tale comparative layer lives — and where the Vita Merlini, with its Morgen and its Island of Apples, anchors the deep-mythic root of the Arthurian tree.
