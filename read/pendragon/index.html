The oldest tale

Culhwch ac Olwen

The earliest Arthurian story we have (c. 1100), preserved in the Welsh Mabinogion. The boy born in a pig-run, the giant whose eyebrows are propped on forks, the council of the world's oldest animals, and the hunt for the great boar Twrch Trwyth.

Pwyll Pendefig Dyfed

The First Branch of the Mabinogi — a foundational Welsh pre-Arthurian tale of exchanges: Pwyll trades places with Arawn lord of Annwn, meets Rhiannon on the Gorsedd mound and outwits Gwawl with the badger-in-the-bag, and recovers his stolen son Pryderi by way of Teirnon's hand on the foal-stealing claw. The complete 7,600-word Middle Welsh tale from the manuscript tradition, in original English translation alongside the Welsh. Annotation layer to follow.

Owain, neu Iarlles y Ffynnon

The Lady of the Fountain — one of the Three Welsh Romances and the Brittonic twin of Chrétien's Yvain: a knight of Arthur's court rides to a storm-making fountain, defeats its Black Knight and weds the widowed Lady, breaks the term she set and runs mad in the wilderness, and is redeemed with the help of a grateful lion. The complete Middle Welsh tale from the Red Book of Hergest (Rhys & Evans 1887), in original English translation across all nine movements, with the full annotation layer — characters, story graph, motif index, mythograph and a storybook.

Branwen ferch Llŷr

The Second Branch of the Mabinogi — the great Welsh tragedy, and the sister-tale of Pwyll. The giant king Bendigeidfran marries his sister Branwen to Ireland to bind the two islands; his half-brother Efnisien's malice turns the alliance to war; the Cauldron of Rebirth gives back the dead but dumb; and Brân's living severed head feasts the seven survivors for fourscore years. Method

What we mean by reading a tale deeply, why we mean it, and how a seventh tale would be added to the cycle.

The apparatus

Every tale lives at its own sub-site of read.mino.mobi as a self-contained reader. Each one is built from the same seven layers, and each layer is data first — JavaScript objects, not narrative HTML — so the rendering, the comparisons, and the eventual port to anything else are mechanical.

1

Source text

A reading text in the tale's original language — Middle Welsh for the Mabinogion branches, Middle English for the Pearl Poet and the Breton lays. Public-domain or CC-BY-SA, with manuscript provenance noted. The source is sacred; we never paraphrase it away.

2

Original translation

Written movement by movement (passage by passage, fitt by fitt, branch by branch), in segment-aligned parallel with the source. Tuned for readability, not for the philological journals; a footnote layer carries the choices that matter. The Read tab toggles between parallel, English-only and source-only modes.

3

Character cards & web

Every named figure as a card — role, epithet, blurb, appearances by movement, typed relationships to other characters. The same data, force-laid as a graph, becomes the Character web. Click a node, jump to a card; click an appearance, jump to the passage.

4

Story graph (Propp)

The tale laid against Vladimir Propp's 31 narrative functions (Α, β, γ, δ, ε, ζ, …): which functions fire, which are absent, which the tale rearranges. Each function links back to the movement that realises it. The same scaffolding works for every folk-rooted narrative — that's exactly Propp's claim.

5

Motif index

The tale by call-number — Stith Thompson's Motif-Index of Folk Literature and the Aarne-Thompson-Uther tale-type catalogue, the folklorists' Dewey decimal for mythic memes. Each motif carries a confidence (well-attested / interpretive / speculative) and the movements where it shows.

6

Mythograph

One synergistic graph over everything above. Movements pin to a horizontal gold spine; characters, motifs and Propp functions hang off the moments they touch, force-laid so cross-cutting elements pull toward the centre. Hover any node; click any node. It's the index of indexes.

7

Storybook

A faithful retelling for readers ~8 and up — the whole tale across paged spreads, with art briefs for each illustration. The storybook keeps the apparatus honest: if a tale doesn't fit a child-readable page-by-page retelling, the deeper layers are masking confusion. Images are generated from the briefs by a shared illustration pipeline; the artwork is data too.

The shape of the apparatus

Each tale-site stands alone — you can read Culhwch without ever knowing the Pearl Poet exists. But because every layer is structured the same way, the comparative layer at Pendragon can read across them all without lifting a finger: the same Propp function code points at six different realisations, the same motif call-number cross-cuts every cycle, the same character archetype appears under six faces. That's the spine of this site.

The apparatus is intentionally read-only at the cross-tale level. The single source of truth for each tale is its own sub-site's data files; Pendragon picks them up and arranges them.

The illustration pipeline

One registry, six tales. Adding the illustrations for a new tale used to mean a new script. Now it's a record in scripts/illustrate/tales.mjs — a few sentences of house style, a dictionary of character & setting "pins" with the descriptions to inject when each appears, a list of regex triggers that match those pins to a given spread's art brief. A single runner, scripts/illustrate.mjs, takes --tale <slug> and renders only the spreads whose PNGs are missing — safe to re-run on every push. A matrix workflow fans it out across every registered tale.

The point is that the artwork is consequential of the writing. The storybook's illus field is an art note — the illustrator (human or model) reads it; the pipeline assembles a prompt by selecting whichever pins the brief mentions; the picture comes out matching the prose. Change a brief, delete the PNG, push: the image is rebuilt to match.

The vision

A tale read deeply once is data forever. The annotation layer is the durable contribution — the place where attention compounds. Every motif we pin and every Propp function we mark is a tiny piece of literary infrastructure that the next tale, and the comparative layer, get to reuse.

The six tales here are the proof of concept that the same scaffolding works on a 12th-century Welsh prose tale (Culhwch), a 14th-century alliterative romance (Gawain), a c.1300 Breton lay (Orfeo), the first two Branches of the Mabinogi (Pwyll and the tragic Branwen), and one of the Three Welsh Romances — the Welsh twin of Chrétien’s Yvain — (Owain). The interesting work begins when the corpus is large enough to surface its own patterns: which motifs travel across the British Isles and which stop at the Welsh border; which Propp functions the Arthurian romances elide and which the Mabinogion branches keep; which character archetypes wear new faces in every century. The Mythograph is the inside-one-tale view of that; Pendragon is the across-tales view.

How to add a sixth tale

The pattern, in the order that has worked six times:

  1. Pick a tale, find a public-domain reading text in the original language. Welsh Wikisource, the TEAMS Middle English series, the Camelot Project, Gutenberg — note the manuscript line in the meta block.
  2. Scaffold the sub-site at read/<slug>/: index.html, styles.css, an empty tale.js with the passages skeleton, and the standard app.js view-switching glue copied from the most recent sister.
  3. Translate movement by movement. Each push to a movement deploys; review on the live site. Pwyll took six commits.
  4. Write characters.js — every named figure as a card, with role, blurb, appearances and typed relationships. The Character web falls out for free.
  5. Write analysis.js — the Propp pass. Where Propp's 31 functions fire, which are absent, which the tale shifts.
  6. Write motifs.js — Thompson motifs and ATU types, with confidence levels and cross-tale references.
  7. Add the Mythograph hookup to app.js; it builds itself from the four data files above.
  8. Write storybook.js — the faithful retelling, each spread carrying a title, prose text and an illus art brief.
  9. Register the tale in scripts/illustrate/tales.mjs — house style, character pins, regex triggers. Add the slug to the workflow matrix.
  10. Add a card to the Pendragon home page and confirm the cross-tale crosswalk (motifs and Propp functions) picks up the new entries.

The sixth tale, Branwen ferch Llŷr — the Second Branch, the corpus’s one tragedy — is now complete and wired into the crosswalk above, bringing the Cauldron of Rebirth and Bran’s talking head (the older cousin of the Green Knight’s) into the comparison. The natural seventh is either a sibling of Branwen that carries on the Four Branches — Manawydan (the Third, the direct sequel) or Math (the Fourth) — or a sibling of Owain that completes the Three Welsh Romances and extends the Welsh↔Continental axis: Peredur (the Welsh Perceval, the Grail) or Geraint (the Welsh Erec). The apparatus is ready.

Who was Constantine III?

Flavius Claudius Constantinus was, by the scraps that survive, an ordinary soldier with a very lucky name. In 407 the Roman army in Britain — twitchy, underpaid, watching the Saxons come and the empire look away — mutinied through three would-be emperors in quick succession and settled on him, reportedly because Constantine was an auspicious name to conjure with (the great Constantine had himself been hailed emperor on British soil, at York, a hundred years before). He took the British field army across the Channel, fought through Gaul, and made his capital at Arelate — modern Arles, on the warm rim of the Mediterranean. For about four years (407–411) he ruled Gaul and Spain as Augustus, was grudgingly recognised by the legitimate western emperor Honorius and then un-recognised, besieged at Arles, and finally captured and executed in 411. Stripping the garrison out is part of why Britain was left to fend for itself — the moment (the Rescript of Honorius, 410) when the empire told the British cities to look to their own defence.

What it gets uncannily right

Run down the checklist and it is startling:

  • A common soldier raised by mutinous British legions ✔
  • Invades Gaul, marches south, declares himself emperor ✔
  • A Mediterranean palace at Arles ✔ — plus the lovely human point that to a rain-soaked Briton the Riviera is simply paradise, the kind of memory that swells in the retelling ✔
  • Rome too distracted to stop him ✔ (just not by Constantinople — see below)

The kicker: when Geoffrey of Monmouth finally gave Arthur his full biography in 1136, he had him do this very thing — conquer Gaul, kill a Roman official, and march on Rome to be crowned emperor, only for treason at home to call him back. The shape of Geoffrey's Arthur is the shape of a British usurper's Gaulish campaign.

Where it wobbles (fun, not rigor)

Chronology. Constantine III dies in 411. The Arthur of legend is pinned a couple of generations later — Badon around 500, Camlann around 537 (see the In-world timeline). So he is less “Arthur” than the doorway Arthur walks through: his revolt is literally the 410 end-of-Roman-Britain moment the whole legend is set just after.

“Rome too busy fighting Constantinople.” Gently: the western court at Ravenna wasn't at war with the eastern capital — East and West were nominally one empire with two courts. It was busy being sacked by Alaric's Goths in 410 and juggling other usurpers. But “Rome had bigger problems” is dead right.

You're not the first — and the pattern is real

The instinct is sound, and there's a well-trodden version of it. The clearest case of a British-army usurper becoming legend isn't Constantine III but Magnus Maximus — proclaimed by the British legions in 383, invaded Gaul, ruled the West from Trier until 388. The Welsh remembered him as Macsen Wledig, gave him a dreamy romance in the Mabinogion (The Dream of Macsen), and planted him as a founding ancestor at the head of medieval Welsh royal genealogies. That's the template this theory is reaching for — and Constantine III is the very next iteration of it, one generation on, with the bonus of the Arles palace.

And for an actual “Arthur was a Briton who invaded Gaul” thesis, meet Riothamus — a 5th-century “king of the Britons” who really did ship an army across to Gaul around 470, was betrayed, and vanished after a defeat near a Burgundian town called… Avallon. Geoffrey Ashe built a whole book (The Discovery of King Arthur, 1985) on identifying him with Arthur. The Avalon echo writes itself.

The Arles coda

The best touch is the palace. Constantine's Arelate was a genuine imperial seat on the Mediterranean; the south of France is still, to a Briton, a vision of warmth and ease. You can absolutely imagine veterans limping back to the rain with tales of a British general who took the legions south, made himself emperor, and held court beside a turquoise sea — and you can imagine those tales, retold for three centuries, hardening into a once-and-future king. Whether or not it's true, it's how legends actually form. (See the historical-Arthur question for how thin the real evidence is.)

Verdict — Vibes: A. Rigor: C–. The specific Constantine III attribution is a stretch on the dates, but the underlying instinct — British legions + a jumped-up soldier + a Gaulish empire + stories carried home — is exactly how the Welsh turned Magnus Maximus into Macsen Wledig and how Geoffrey built his conquering Arthur. Worth the price of admission for the Arles palace alone.
