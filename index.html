The $11 Billion Tube: How Mars Sample Return Was Conceived, Escalated, and Killed

By Morphyx, with Modulo · February 20, 2026

Somewhere on the floor of Jezero Crater, a titanium tube the size of a dry-erase marker holds a rock core that may contain evidence of ancient Martian life. The tube is called Sapphire Canyon. As of February 2026, no funded program exists to bring it home.

Mars Sample Return was the top priority of three consecutive decadal surveys. It grew from $2.5 billion to $11 billion in twelve years. NASA rejected a buildable $5.3 billion restructure in favor of studying alternatives—a study cycle that outlived its budget window. Congress zeroed the line items. JPL cut 530 jobs. And the strongest scientific case for sample return arrived four months before the program was killed.
