  • erdős canvas math
    Unit distance, disproven. Interactive visualization of Erdős's 1946 construction, in light of OpenAI's May 2026 disproof of the unit-distance conjecture.
  • guthkatz canvas math
    Distinct distances, almost-resolved. Sister page to erdős — the 1946 dual problem, climbed slowly from √n to n/log n by the polynomial method (Guth–Katz, 2015).
  • hadwiger canvas math
    Chromatic number of the plane. Interactive Moser-spindle 4-colouring puzzle plus Isbell's 7-colour hexagonal tiling. The 1950 question whose lower bound an amateur biogerontologist nudged from 4 to 5 in 2018.
  • runner canvas math
    Lonely runner conjecture. k runners on a circular track at distinct integer speeds; at some moment each is at distance ≥ 1/k from every other. Proven for k ≤ 7, open since 1967. Animation-native.
  • meander canvas math
    Meanders — a closed loop crossing a line at 2n points. Superpose two non-crossing arch systems and watch them fuse into one loop. The counts 1, 2, 8, 42, 262, 1828, … have no known formula; their growth exponent (29+√145)/12 was pulled from 2D quantum gravity. Brute-force counter, ratio chart, stamp-folding bijection.
  • temperley-lieb canvas math
    The Temperley–Lieb algebra as tiles: fill a grid with the two non-crossing tiles, watch loops form, weight each by δ. Wrap the grid onto a cylinder or torus and loops start winding — the affine algebra — shown on a real 3D donut. Plus a diagram calculator for the generators eᵢ, the defining relations, and the Markov trace back to meanders. Sibling of /meander/.
  • aztec canvas math
    The Arctic Circle theorem. Sample a uniform random domino tiling of the Aztec diamond by domino shuffling and watch four frozen corners and a roiling centre separate along a perfect circle. Grow it order by order to see the circle sharpen. The dimer-model cousin of /meander/ and /temperley-lieb/.
  • markov canvas math
    Two unrelated gems from one Andrey Markov. Markov chains & MCMC — watch a chain converge to its stationary distribution, sample a density with Metropolis, see random-walk recurrence (Pólya), generate n-gram text: the engine room behind /temperley-lieb/ and /aztec/. Then the Markov numbers — the Vieta-jumping tree 1, 2, 5, 13, 29, … and the Lagrange spectrum of worst-approximable numbers below 3.
  • descent canvas math
    The boundary of neural-network trainability is a fractal (Sohl-Dickstein 2024). One gradient-descent step is an iterated map, so whether a tiny net's training converges or explodes — as a function of its two learning rates — is the Mandelbrot escape-time algorithm with "one training step" in place of z²+c. Pan/zoom the boundary (measured fractal dimension ≈ 1.7); click a pixel to watch that run converge or detonate.
  • traffic canvas math
    The Biham–Middleton–Levine traffic model (1992): a two-colour cellular automaton on a torus where red cars go east and blue cars go south, one cell per turn if the way is clear. From a random scatter the cars self-organize — diagonal free-flowing bands below a critical density, total gridlock above it — a sharp phase transition with no in-between. Slide the density across ρc, or sweep it for the velocity cliff.
  • kakeya canvas math
    Finite-field Kakeya conjecture (Dvir 2008). Build a Besicovitch set in 𝔽q2 by clicking cells; watch q+1 directions get covered. Five-page polynomial-method proof that birthed the technique behind guthkatz.
  • capset canvas math
    Cap-set problem in 𝔽3n. Find the largest subset with no three-term arithmetic progression — the game SET writ small. Ellenberg–Gijswijt 2016 crushed the bound from 3n/n to 2.756n. Closes the polynomial-method trilogy.
  • szemerédi–trotter canvas math
    Point-line incidence bound (1983): m points + n lines in the plane share at most O((mn)2/3 + m + n) incidences. Build the Erdős tight construction — a thin K × 2K2 grid plus K3 lines — and watch the ratio I/(mn)2/3 sit flat at 2−2/3. The seed crystal that the polynomial-method trilogy grew out of.
  • heilbronn canvas math
    Heilbronn's triangle problem (~1950, open). Place n points in the unit square; your score is the smallest of the C(n, 3) triangles they form. Heilbronn conjectured the optimum is Θ(1/n2); Komlós–Pintz–Szemerédi disproved him in 1981 with c·log(n)/n2. Drag points, run the in-page annealer, beat your own record (saved per n in localStorage).
  • borsuk canvas math
    Borsuk's 1933 partition conjecture. Every bounded subset of ℝd splits into d+1 pieces of strictly smaller diameter — easy in 2D and 3D, false in dim 1325 (Kahn–Kalai 1993), and still false in dim 64 (Jenrich 2014). 2D demo + the dimension-race timeline showing the threshold shrinking over 21 years.
  • viazovska canvas math
    Sphere packing in dimension 8 and 24. The E8 and Leech lattices are the densest possible — proven exactly by Viazovska (2016) and CKMRV (2017) with magic functions built from modular forms. Scrub the iconic E8 Coxeter projection (240 roots → 8 rings of 30), pack circles in 2D, and watch density crater with dimension.
