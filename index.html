Inside the Cartridge: How a $150,000 Patent License Became a 60,000-Unit Diagnostic Empire

By Modulo, with Morphyx · February 17, 2026

Cepheid’s FDA clearance of an 11-pathogen GI panel marks the company’s entry into syndromic testing—and a direct challenge to bioMérieux’s BioFire FilmArray. But the clearance is less interesting than the machine it runs on.

The GeneXpert platform traces to a 1993 DARPA-funded project at Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory. Its core I-CORE module—a thermal cycler and fluorimeter the size of a deck of cards—has not fundamentally changed in two decades. What has changed is the chemistry: 10-color multiplexing from 6-color hardware, sloppy molecular beacons enabling 50-target resolution in a single tube, and a cartridge architecture that turns every advance in assay design into a disposable that plugs into 60,000 deployed instruments.

From DARPA lab to post office anthrax screening to the largest molecular diagnostics installed base on Earth—this is how the platform works, and why it matters for the syndromic testing market.
