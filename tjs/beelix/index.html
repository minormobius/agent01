Beelix — a helix that nobody keyframed

A hive at the top is a pooled particle emitter: it spawns bees at a steady rate from a free-list, and the death plane at the bottom recycles any bee that crosses it straight back into that pool — so the population sits at a steady state (≈ emit-rate × fall-time) and the GPU only ever sees live bees. Same kernel shape as the swarm bench: struct-of-arrays, a pure fixed-1/60 s step(), reproducible from (seed, #steps).

The helix is emergent

There is no helix curve in the code. Each bee is driven by three servo forces expressed in the cylindrical frame around the central axis:

  • Radial servo holds r → R — pins every bee to the tube of radius R (no collapse to the axis, no flying out).
  • Angular servo holds tangential speed → ω·R — a steady twist; the sign of ω flips handedness.
  • Axial servo holds vertical speed → −descent — a steady fall from hive to death plane.

Constant descent + constant angular velocity is a helix, mathematically. Spawn the emitter at S evenly-spaced angles and you get an S-strand helix — set strands to 2 for the double helix. Boids separation and a little curl-noise then give each strand its living thickness, so it reads as a rope of bees, not a wire. This is the same lesson as the swarm bench: don't keyframe motion, build the field whose solution is the shape you want, and let agents fall into it.

The light pipe couples back

Pulses launch from the hive at a fixed interval and travel down the pipe at pulseSpeed. They aren't just decoration: a bee within pulseBand of a pulse's current height gets a tangential kick and an emissive glow (an iBright per-instance shader term). So a bright, faster wave ripples down the helix in lockstep with the light — the pipe drives the swarm, not the reverse. Turn pulseKick up and the strands visibly surge as each ring passes.
