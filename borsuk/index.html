Karol Borsuk · 1933 · false above dim 63

Take a bounded subset of ℝᵈ. Its diameter is the largest distance between any two of its points. Borsuk asked: can we always split it into pieces, each of smaller diameter, and how many pieces do we need?

Borsuk 1933: d+1 pieces always suffice.

This isn't crazy. A disk in the plane splits into 3 sectors of 120° each; each sector has diameter √3·R < 2R. A ball in 3-space splits into 4 pieces using an inscribed regular tetrahedron. Borsuk himself proved it in dim 2; Eggleston proved it in dim 3 using a clever projection argument. The conjecture was on its way.

The Kahn–Kalai breakthrough

Sixty years later, Jeff Kahn and Gil Kalai exhibited a finite set of ±1 vectors in dim 1325 that resists partition into 1326 smaller-diameter pieces. The construction uses the Frankl–Wilson theorem on the rank of certain set-incidence matrices over finite fields. The "set" is the vertices of a Hamming-style configuration where most pairs of vectors achieve the maximum pairwise distance — so any partition must split many such pairs apart, and 1326 isn't enough.

The number of pieces required actually grows faster than any polynomial in d. Kahn and Kalai showed B(d) ≥ (1.2)√d asymptotically; later authors have improved the base.

The dimension race

Once the conjecture was known to fail somewhere, attention turned to the lowest dimension where it does. Each improvement comes from sharpening the combinatorial construction:

  • Kahn–Kalai 1993 · dim 1325
  • Raigorodskii 1997 · dim 561
  • Hinrichs 1999 · dim 323
  • Hinrichs–Richter 2003 · dim 298
  • Bondarenko 2014 · dim 65, two-distance set on a sphere
  • Jenrich 2014 · dim 64, refining Bondarenko

Whether the conjecture fails in any dimension between 4 and 63 is open. For applications in convex geometry it's also open whether smooth convex bodies satisfy the conjecture — the known counterexamples are all finite point sets, which is allowed but feels like cheating.

Connections in the pack

  • Like erdős's unit-distance question (also disproven by an unexpected construction), borsuk shows low-dimensional intuition fails badly in high dim. Both are "the conjecture was a guess, and the guess was wrong."
  • The Frankl–Wilson theorem is also the seed for capset-adjacent finite-field counting arguments; both live in the same combinatorial-geometry toolbox.
  • Unlike heilbronn, where the right answer is unknown but the gap is bounded, borsuk's answer is now known to be unboundedly off from the conjectured d+1.
